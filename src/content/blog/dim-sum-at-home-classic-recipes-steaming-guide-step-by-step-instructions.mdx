Dim sum at home means recreating the traditional Cantonese yum cha experience in your own kitchen — steaming, frying, or baking small, flavourful dishes like har gow (shrimp dumplings), siu mai (pork dumplings), cheung fun (rice noodle rolls), lo mai gai (sticky rice in lotus leaf), and egg tarts. With a bamboo steamer, basic pantry staples, and the right dough techniques, you can serve an authentic spread of homemade dim sum for family or guests.

![Homemade dim sum spread with har gow, siu mai and cheung fun in open bamboo steamers on a dark slate surface](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/dim-sum-at-home-hero-320.webp)

## What Is Yum Cha?

**Yum cha** (飲茶) literally translates to "drink tea" in Cantonese, but the term encompasses far more than a cup of tea. It refers to the beloved Chinese tradition of gathering at a teahouse — or today, a restaurant — to share plates of dim sum while sipping fragrant teas like jasmine, pu-erh, or chrysanthemum. The ritual originated in ancient teahouses along the Silk Road, where travellers would stop to rest and eat small bites alongside tea.

Today, yum cha is a cornerstone of Cantonese culture, typically enjoyed on Sunday mornings with the whole family. Stainless steel trolleys are wheeled between tables, each loaded with bamboo steamers, crisp-fried taro puffs, and sticky rice parcels. The social, unhurried nature of the meal is part of the appeal — dishes arrive continuously, conversation flows, and no one rushes.

**Recreating yum cha at home** has become increasingly popular, and for good reason. It gives you complete control over ingredients, allows dietary customisations (gluten-free wrappers, low-sodium fillings), and turns a meal into a collaborative cooking experience. If you're already exploring the broader world of Chinese cooking, you'll find that **[Chinese Food: The Ultimate Recipe Guide 2026](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/cuisines/chinese/)** provides an excellent roadmap for understanding regional flavour profiles and essential techniques that apply directly to your dim sum spread.

## Essential Equipment: Steamer Setup

Getting your steamer setup right is the single most important factor in making restaurant-quality dim sum at home. Underpowered steam means soggy, sticky, or unevenly cooked dumplings.

### What You Need

**Bamboo Steamers (Recommended)** Bamboo steamers are the authentic choice. They absorb excess moisture, preventing condensation from dripping onto your dumplings — which is the main cause of wet, collapsing wrappers. A 25–30cm (10–12 inch) diameter steamer is ideal for home use, and you can stack two or three tiers over a single wok.

- **Line with parchment paper or napa cabbage leaves** — prevents sticking without blocking steam
- **Leave 2–3cm gaps between dumplings** — steam needs to circulate freely
- **Use boiling water from the start** — never place steamers over cold water

**Metal / Stainless Steel Steamers** These work well and are easier to clean, but you'll need to wrap the lid in a clean tea towel to prevent drips.

**Wok Setup** Fill your wok with about 5–6cm of water. Bring to a rolling boil before placing the bamboo steamer on the rim. Ensure the water level doesn't touch the bottom basket. Replenish with boiling water (never cold) during long cooking sessions.

**Other Useful Tools**

- **Rolling pin** (preferably a thin Chinese-style one for har gow wrappers)
- **Pastry scraper** for dividing dough
- **Kitchen scale** — precision matters for dim sum doughs
- **Silicone brush** for egg wash on baked items
![Bamboo steamer basket, rolling pin, kitchen scale and pastry scraper laid out on grey concrete for making dim sum at home](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/dim-sum-steamer-setup-equipment-320.webp)

## Dim Sum Dough Tips

The doughs used in dim sum vary significantly by dish, and understanding the differences is key to authentic results.

### Wheat Starch Dough (Har Gow Wrapper)

This creates the glossy, translucent wrappers that distinguish true har gow from ordinary dumplings. It uses **wheat starch** (澄面, cheng mian) — not plain flour — combined with tapioca starch for elasticity.

**The key rule:** Use boiling water only. Pouring hot water over wheat starch gelatinises the starch, creating a pliable, translucent dough. Cold water will not work.

**Basic ratio:** 100g wheat starch + 35g tapioca starch + 170ml boiling water + 1 tsp vegetable oil

- Knead while warm (use food-safe gloves)
- Rest covered for 10 minutes
- Divide into small balls (about 12–15g each) and flatten with a cleaver or rolling pin
- Work quickly — the dough dries out fast
![Smooth warm wheat starch dough ball in a white ceramic bowl ready for shaping har gow wrappers](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Smooth-warm-wheat-starch-dough-ball-in-a-white-ceramic-bowl-ready-for-shaping-har-gow-wrappers-320.webp)

### Pork/Shrimp Filling Tips

- **Mince by hand** rather than blending for better texture
- Add **baking soda (¼ tsp per 200g shrimp)** for that bouncy, restaurant-style snap
- Always **marinate fillings for at least 30 minutes** in the fridge before filling dumplings
- A small amount of **sesame oil and white pepper** is non-negotiable for authentic flavour

### Rice Batter (Cheung Fun)

Rice noodle rolls use a thin, pourable batter of **rice flour and tapioca starch**, spread onto an oiled tray and steamed flat. The ratio of rice flour to water determines texture — too thick and it's gluey; too thin and it tears.

## Har Gow Recipe (Steamed Shrimp Dumplings)

**Har gow** (蝦餃) is widely considered the benchmark of any dim sum restaurant. A good har gow has a thin, translucent, slightly chewy wrapper that pleads at the edges and a plump, juicy prawn filling. If a restaurant's har gow is excellent, the rest of the menu will be too.

### Ingredients (Makes 24 pieces)

**Wrapper Dough:**

- 100g wheat starch (cheng mian)
- 35g tapioca starch
- 170ml boiling water
- 1 tsp vegetable oil
- Pinch of salt

**Filling:**

- 300g raw prawns, peeled and deveined
- 80g bamboo shoots, finely diced
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tsp soy sauce
- ½ tsp sugar
- ¼ tsp white pepper
- ¼ tsp baking soda
- 1 tsp cornstarch

![Six freshly steamed har gow dumplings with translucent pleated wrappers and pink prawn filling in a bamboo steamer](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/har-gow-steamed-shrimp-dumplings-recipe-320.webp)

### Method

**Step 1 — Make the Filling** Pat prawns dry. Set aside 100g and roughly chop. Finely mince the remaining 200g by rocking a cleaver back and forth until almost paste-like. Combine both textures — this gives a mix of snap and substance. Add bamboo shoots, sesame oil, soy sauce, sugar, white pepper, baking soda, and cornstarch. Mix vigorously in one direction for 2 minutes until sticky. Refrigerate for 30 minutes.

**Step 2 — Make the Dough** Combine wheat starch, tapioca starch, and salt in a heatproof bowl. Pour boiling water in all at once and stir immediately with a spatula until a rough dough forms. Add oil, then knead (wear gloves — it's hot) for 2–3 minutes until smooth. Cover with a damp cloth.

**Step 3 — Shape** Divide dough into 24 equal pieces (~12g each). Flatten each into a thin disc (about 8cm) using a lightly oiled cleaver or rolling pin. Place 1 tsp filling in the centre. Fold one side over the filling and pleat 5–7 folds along the edge to seal. The finished dumpling should have a crescent shape.

**Step 4 — Steam** Line steamer baskets with parchment. Place har gow 2cm apart. Steam over vigorously boiling water for 7–9 minutes until wrappers are translucent and filling is cooked through.

**Serve** with a dipping sauce of soy sauce, ginger, and a few drops of chilli oil.

> **Pro Tip:** Lightly oil your hands and the work surface to prevent the wheat starch dough from sticking. Speed matters — shape and steam in batches.

## Siu Mai Recipe (Open-Top Pork Dumplings)

![ Six open-topped siu mai dumplings with yellow wonton wrappers and pork prawn filling topped with orange tobiko in a bamboo steamer](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/siu-mai-pork-prawn-dumpling-recipe-320.webp)

**Siu mai** (燒賣) are open-topped, cylindrical dumplings with a yellow wonton-style wrapper, filled with a mixture of pork and prawns. A single tobiko fish roe or a frozen pea on top is the traditional garnish. They're arguably the most forgiving of the classic dim sum pieces for home cooks, because the filling is more flexible and the shaping is simpler.

For more variety in your dumpling repertoire, including regional variations you may not know, the **[Chinese Dumplings: Every Style from Jiaozi to Xiaolongbao](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/blog/)** guide covers the full taxonomy of Chinese dumplings and how each differs in dough, filling, and cooking technique.

### Ingredients (Makes 24 pieces)

**Wrappers:**

- 24 round wonton wrappers (store-bought yellow wrappers, ~7.5cm)

**Filling:**

- 300g fatty pork mince (30% fat content)
- 150g raw prawns, roughly chopped
- 4 dried shiitake mushrooms, soaked and finely diced
- 2 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tbsp Shaoxing rice wine
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- ½ tsp sugar
- ½ tsp white pepper
- 1 tbsp cornstarch
- 1 tsp soy sauce

**To Garnish:**

- Tobiko (flying fish roe), frozen peas, or finely diced carrot

### Method

**Step 1 — Make the Filling** Combine pork mince, chopped prawns, and diced mushrooms in a bowl. Add oyster sauce, Shaoxing wine, sesame oil, sugar, white pepper, cornstarch, and soy sauce. Stir vigorously in one direction for 3–4 minutes until the mixture becomes sticky and cohesive. Refrigerate for 20 minutes.

**Step 2 — Shape** Hold a wonton wrapper in your non-dominant hand. Place a rounded tablespoon of filling in the centre. Curl your fingers around the wrapper, using your thumb and forefinger to form a "waist," gently squeezing to create the cylindrical shape. The filling should sit proud above the wrapper rim. Flatten the base by tapping gently on the work surface so the siu mai stands upright.

**Step 3 — Garnish and Steam** Place a single piece of tobiko, a pea, or a tiny carrot cube in the centre of each. Line bamboo steamer with parchment and place siu mai 1–2cm apart. Steam over boiling water for 8–10 minutes.

**Serve** with soy sauce, chilli oil, and XO sauce on the side.

## Cheung Fun Recipe (Steamed Rice Noodle Rolls)

![ Four freshly rolled cheung fun rice noodle rolls on a white plate drizzled with dark sweet soy sauce and sesame seeds](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/cheung-fun-steamed-rice-noodle-rolls-320.webp)

**Cheung fun** (腸粉) are silky, slippery rice noodle rolls that can be served plain with sweet soy sauce, or filled with prawns, beef, or char siu (BBQ pork). The texture should be almost gossamer-thin — smooth, slightly sticky on the outside, and yielding when you bite through.

According to **[The Spruce Eats](https://www.thespruceeats.com/)**, rice noodle rolls are one of the most delicate and technique-dependent dishes in Cantonese cuisine, with the batter ratio and steaming surface temperature being the two most critical variables.

### Ingredients (Makes 8 rolls)

**Batter:**

- 150g rice flour
- 30g tapioca starch
- 20g cornstarch
- 500ml cold water
- ½ tsp salt
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil

**Prawn Filling (Optional):**

- 200g raw prawns, peeled and halved lengthways
- 1 tsp light soy sauce
- ½ tsp sesame oil

**Serving Sauce:**

- 3 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tbsp sugar
- 3 tbsp water
- 1 tsp sesame oil

### Method

**Step 1 — Prepare the Batter** Whisk together rice flour, tapioca starch, cornstarch, salt, and cold water until completely smooth and lump-free. Add vegetable oil. Rest for 15 minutes. Strain through a fine sieve.

**Step 2 — Set Up the Steam Tray** You'll need a flat baking tray (about 25 x 35cm) that fits inside your steamer, or use a dedicated cheung fun tray. Brush generously with oil.

**Step 3 — Steam** Bring your steamer to a vigorous boil. Pour a thin layer of batter (~80ml) onto the oiled tray — it should barely cover the surface. Scatter a few halved prawns across the batter if using. Cover and steam for 4–5 minutes until the sheet is set (it will look slightly translucent with no liquid pools).

**Step 4 — Roll** Carefully remove the tray from the steamer. Use a wide spatula to roll the noodle sheet from one end to the other, working quickly before it cools. Place seam-side down on a plate. Repeat with remaining batter.

**Step 5 — Sauce and Serve** Heat the serving sauce ingredients together until sugar dissolves. Pour over rolls and serve immediately with sesame seeds and chopped spring onion.

## Lo Mai Gai Recipe (Sticky Rice in Lotus Leaf)

![Unwrapped lo mai gai sticky rice parcel in lotus leaf revealing chicken, Chinese sausage, mushroom and dried shrimp filling](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/lo-mai-gai-sticky-rice-lotus-leaf-recipe-320.webp)

**Lo mai gai** (糯米雞) are one of the most satisfying and substantial items in any dim sum spread — parcels of glutinous sticky rice packed with chicken, Chinese sausage, mushrooms, and dried shrimp, all wrapped in fragrant dried lotus leaves. The lotus leaf perfumes the rice as it steams, adding a uniquely earthy, grassy aroma you simply cannot replicate any other way.

This is a dish where advance preparation pays dividends. The rice needs soaking, the lotus leaves need soaking, and the filling needs to be cooked before assembly — but the actual parcels can be made the day before and refrigerated until needed.

### Ingredients (Makes 6 parcels)

**Sticky Rice:**

- 400g glutinous rice, soaked overnight and drained
- 2 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tbsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 150ml chicken stock

**Filling:**

- 2 chicken thighs, boneless, cut into 2cm pieces
- 2 Chinese sausages (lap cheong), sliced
- 6 dried shiitake mushrooms, soaked and quartered
- 2 tbsp dried shrimp, soaked
- 3 salted egg yolks, halved (optional)

**Chicken Marinade:**

- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1 tsp cornstarch
- ½ tsp white pepper
- 1 tsp sesame oil

**Other:**

- 3 large dried lotus leaves, soaked in warm water for 1 hour, cut in half

### Method

**Step 1 — Cook the Rice** Drain soaked glutinous rice. Steam over boiling water for 25 minutes until just cooked but still slightly firm. Transfer to a bowl and toss with oyster sauce, dark soy sauce, sesame oil, and chicken stock while hot. The rice should absorb all the liquid.

**Step 2 — Prepare the Filling** Marinate chicken pieces for at least 30 minutes. Stir-fry in a wok over high heat with a little oil for 3–4 minutes until just cooked. Add Chinese sausage, mushrooms, and drained dried shrimp and toss together for 1–2 minutes. Set aside.

**Step 3 — Assemble** Lay a lotus leaf half, rough side up, on your work surface. Place a generous mound of sticky rice (about 4 tbsp) in the centre. Create a well and add 2–3 tbsp of the filling. Top with a piece of salted egg yolk if using. Cover completely with another mound of sticky rice (4 tbsp), pressing firmly to compact the parcel. Fold the lotus leaf tightly around the rice to form a neat rectangle or square parcel. Secure with kitchen twine if needed.

**Step 4 — Steam** Place parcels seam-side down in your bamboo steamer. Steam over vigorous boiling water for 40–45 minutes. Replenish water as needed. Serve hot, unwrapping at the table for the full aromatic effect.

## Chinese Egg Tarts (Dan Tat)

![Four freshly baked Chinese egg tarts with golden pastry shells and smooth pale yellow custard filling on a white ceramic plate](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/chinese-egg-tarts-dan-tat-recipe-320.webp)

**Dan tat** (蛋撻) are the most beloved dessert in any yum cha spread — a buttery, flaky or shortcrust pastry shell filled with a smooth, barely-set egg custard. There are two styles: the **Cantonese-style** with a flaky water-dough pastry shell, and the **Hong Kong-style** (influenced by Portuguese pastel de nata) with a crumblier shortcrust base. Both are excellent.

Understanding the egg-to-milk ratio is the secret to a silky, non-watery custard. According to **[BBC Good Food's pastry and custard guides](https://www.bbcgoodfood.com/howto/guide/baking)**, the ideal egg custard for tarts requires balancing whole eggs with evaporated milk and a light sugar syrup to achieve that signature wobble.

### Ingredients (Makes 12 tarts)

**Shortcrust Pastry:**

- 200g plain flour
- 100g cold butter, cubed
- 30g icing sugar
- 1 egg yolk
- 2–3 tbsp cold water

**Egg Custard Filling:**

- 3 large eggs
- 100g caster sugar
- 250ml evaporated milk
- 100ml hot water
- ½ tsp vanilla extract

### Method

**Step 1 — Make the Pastry** Rub cold butter into flour and icing sugar until the mixture resembles breadcrumbs. Add egg yolk and just enough cold water to bring the dough together. Do not overwork. Wrap and refrigerate for 30 minutes.

**Step 2 — Line the Tins** Divide dough into 12 equal pieces. Press each piece into a greased 7cm tart tin, working the dough up the sides evenly to a thickness of about 3mm. Prick the base several times with a fork. Refrigerate for another 15 minutes.

**Step 3 — Make the Custard** Dissolve sugar in hot water and allow to cool. Beat eggs lightly — do not create foam or bubbles. Gently whisk cooled syrup, evaporated milk, and vanilla into the eggs. Strain twice through a fine sieve. The custard should be completely bubble-free for a smooth, blemish-free top.

**Step 4 — Bake** Preheat oven to 200°C (fan 180°C / 390°F). Pour custard into tart shells to about 80% full. Bake for 15–18 minutes until the pastry is golden and the custard is set at the edges but still has a slight wobble in the centre. Do not overbake — the custard continues to set as it cools.

Cool slightly before serving. Egg tarts are best eaten warm.

## Dim Sum Dipping Sauces

![Four dim sum dipping sauces in white ceramic bowls including soy ginger, chilli oil, XO sauce and sweet soy on grey concrete](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/dim-sum-dipping-sauces-four-varieties-320.webp)

No dim sum spread is complete without a selection of sauces. Here are four essential accompaniments:

**Classic Soy-Ginger Dip** Mix 3 tbsp light soy sauce, 1 tsp toasted sesame oil, 1 tsp rice vinegar, and 1 tbsp finely julienned fresh ginger. A staple for har gow and siu mai.

**Chilli Oil** Combine 4 tbsp neutral oil (heated to 180°C), 2 tbsp chilli flakes, 1 tsp Sichuan peppercorns, 1 tsp sesame seeds, and a pinch of salt. Pour hot oil over dry ingredients. Cool before serving.

**XO Sauce** A more complex condiment made from dried scallops, dried shrimp, chilli, and garlic. Store-bought XO sauce is perfectly acceptable for home use — look for brands like Lee Kum Kee or Kimlan.

**Sweet Soy Sauce (for Cheung Fun)** Mix 3 tbsp light soy, 1 tbsp oyster sauce, 1 tbsp sugar, 3 tbsp water, and 1 tsp sesame oil. Heat gently until sugar dissolves.

## Dim Sum Party Planning Tips

If you're hosting a full yum cha spread at home, planning the order of preparation is essential to avoid kitchen chaos.

**Day Before:**

- Soak glutinous rice and lotus leaves for lo mai gai
- Prepare and refrigerate all fillings
- Make pastry for egg tarts

**Morning Of:**

- Assemble and steam lo mai gai (these keep warm easily)
- Prepare cheung fun batter and rest
- Shape har gow and siu mai, refrigerate uncovered on lined trays

**30 Minutes Before Serving:**

- Bake egg tarts
- Begin steaming har gow and siu mai in batches
- Steam cheung fun to order

## FAQ

### What are the best dim sum recipes to make at home for beginners?

If you're new to homemade dim sum, start with **siu mai** — open-topped pork dumplings that use store-bought wonton wrappers and require no special dough skills. **Egg tarts** are equally forgiving and need only basic pastry technique. Once confident, move to **har gow**, which introduces wheat starch dough. Save **lo mai gai** and **cheung fun** for when you're comfortable with steamer setup and timing. All five recipes in this guide include step-by-step instructions designed for home cooks at every level.

### What equipment do I need to make dim sum at home?

You need a bamboo steamer (25–30cm diameter), a wok or large pot, parchment paper or napa cabbage leaves for lining, and a rolling pin. For cheung fun, a flat baking tray that fits inside your steamer is essential. Most other tools — bowls, a knife, a pastry scraper — are standard kitchen items.

### Can I use store-bought wrappers for dim sum?

Yes, for siu mai, store-bought round yellow wonton wrappers work well. However, for har gow, the wheat starch dough cannot be substituted — it is a fundamental part of the texture and appearance. Pre-made cheung fun sheets are available at some Asian supermarkets if you prefer a shortcut.

### How do I prevent dumplings from sticking to the steamer?

Line steamer baskets with parchment paper (punch a few holes for steam circulation) or fresh napa cabbage leaves. Lightly brush the lining with neutral oil before placing dumplings. Never place dumplings directly on bare bamboo.

### Why are my har gow wrappers not translucent?

This usually means the wrappers are too thick, or wheat starch quality is low. Roll wrappers to about 1–1.5mm thickness. Also ensure you are using wheat starch (澄面 / cheng mian), not wheat flour — they are completely different ingredients.

### What tea should I serve with homemade dim sum?

Pu-erh tea is the traditional pairing — its earthy depth cuts through fatty fillings. Jasmine tea is lighter and more floral, pairing well with seafood-based dishes. Chrysanthemum tea is refreshing and slightly sweet, making it ideal for palate-cleansing between courses.

### Can dim sum recipes at home be made ahead of time?

Yes. Assembled but unsteamed har gow and siu mai can be refrigerated uncovered for up to 4 hours, or frozen on a tray before transferring to freezer bags. Steam from frozen, adding 3–4 extra minutes. Lo mai gai parcels can be assembled and refrigerated overnight.

### How many dim sum pieces should I make per person?

For a complete yum cha spread at home, plan for 3–4 pieces of each dish per person. A spread of 4–5 different items for 4 people requires roughly 60–80 individual pieces total. Prioritise 2–3 labour-intensive items (har gow, siu mai) and balance with simpler dishes (cheung fun, egg tarts).

### What is the difference between dim sum and yum cha?

Dim sum (點心) refers to the food itself — the small, individual dishes. Yum cha (飲茶) is the social act of eating dim sum while drinking tea. In everyday conversation the terms are often used interchangeably, but technically you "do yum cha" by eating "dim sum."
