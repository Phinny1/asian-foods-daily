**Jangjorim (장조림)** is a Korean soy-braised beef banchan (side dish) made by simmering lean beef in a seasoned soy sauce broth until tender, then shredding it and serving it cold or at room temperature. It is salty, mildly sweet, deeply savory, and typically accompanied by boiled eggs and shishito peppers. It keeps refrigerated for up to two weeks, making it one of Korea's most practical everyday side dishes (*mitbanchan*).

## What Is **Jangjorim (장조림)?**

Jangjorim is a Korean side dish consisting of lean beef braised in soy sauce with eggs. It is a type of *jorim* — a Korean simmered dish that preserves well — and is commonly packed in lunch boxes and sold at South Korean convenience stores. The dish has deep historical roots: the first known written description appears in Volume 128 of the [Veritable Records of the Joseon Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jang-jorim), referring to a dish called *damhae* made by slicing beef and braising it in soy sauce.

The word *jang* (장) means soy sauce, and *jorim* (조림) refers to a Korean cooking method of reducing liquid into a concentrated glaze. Together they describe exactly what this dish is: meat braised down in a rich, soy-forward sauce until every fibre is saturated with flavour.

## Why This Recipe Works

Jangjorim has a few critical turning points that separate a tender, flavourful result from a tough, over-salted one.

**The Two-Stage Process — How the Timing Works**

```
STAGE 1 — WATER STAGE              STAGE 2 — SOY STAGE
────────────────────────────────    ────────────────────────────────
Pre-soak beef 30 min                Add soy sauce + mirin + sugar
↓                                   ↓
Simmer in aromatics 45–50 min       Simmer uncovered 15 min
↓                                   ↓
Beef tender, broth clean            Sauce reduces to glossy coat
↓                                   ↓
Strain broth, reserve 1 cup         Add eggs + peppers last 10 min
```

**Recipe Testing Notes**

- **Batch 1 — Soy from the start:** Added soy sauce at the beginning. The salt drew moisture out before fibres could soften. Result: visibly tougher, chewy meat. *Failure documented.*
- **Batch 2 — Pre-soak skipped:** Broth was cloudier with a gamey note that carried through into the final sauce. *Suboptimal.*
- **Batch 3 — Two-stage method introduced:** Simmered in plain aromatics first, added soy only after the meat was mostly tender. Noticeably more tender, much cleaner broth. *Significant improvement.*
- **Batch 4 — Sugar added too early:** Sauce caramelised and darkened on the exterior while the interior of the beef remained bland. *Failure documented.*
- **Batch 5 — Optimal:** Pre-soak 30 min → aromatics simmer 45–50 min → soy + mirin + sugar added → eggs and peppers last 10 min. Glossy, evenly flavoured result. *Optimal.*

## **Jangjorim (장조림)** Ingredients

- **500g (1.1 lb) beef brisket** — or eye round, flank steak, or shank
- **5 cups cold water** — for initial simmering
- **½ medium onion** — quartered
- **4 garlic cloves** — whole
- **1-inch piece fresh ginger** — sliced
- **1 bay leaf**
- **1 tsp whole black peppercorns**
- **4 tbsp soy sauce** (regular or slightly reduced-sodium)
- **2 tbsp mirin** (or rice wine)
- **1 tbsp sugar** (or honey)
- **4 large eggs** — hard-boiled and peeled
- **8–10 shishito peppers** (or padron peppers)

## Equipment

- **Medium heavy-bottomed pot (3–4 qt):** A Dutch oven or stainless saucepan works best. The heavier base prevents hot spots that can scorch the sauce during reduction. Avoid thin aluminium pots.
- **Fine-mesh strainer:** Essential for straining the aromatic broth before adding soy sauce. A colander leaves too many solids behind.
- **Kitchen tongs:** For removing beef and cracking boiled egg shells lightly to encourage sauce absorption.
- **Airtight glass container:** Glass doesn't absorb odours and lets you monitor the sauce level without opening the lid.

## How to Make **Jangjorim** (Step-by-Step)

1. **Soak the beef.** Place brisket in cold water for 30 minutes, changing the water once at 15 minutes. This draws out blood and myoglobin — the protein responsible for cloudy, gamey broth.
2. **Build your broth.** Drain soaking water. Add 5 cups fresh cold water with the onion, garlic, ginger, bay leaf, and peppercorns. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat.
3. **Simmer the beef.** Lower heat to medium, add the beef, and skim off any foam. Cover loosely and cook 45–50 minutes until tender but not yet falling apart.
4. **Strain and reserve broth.** Remove beef and set aside. Strain broth through a fine-mesh sieve, discarding solids. Reserve 1 cup of broth.
5. **Add the seasoning.** Return reserved broth to the pot. Add soy sauce, mirin, and sugar. Bring to a gentle simmer.
6. **Return the beef.** Add beef back and simmer uncovered 15 minutes, turning occasionally, until sauce reduces and coats the meat.
7. **Add eggs and peppers.** Nestle the peeled hard-boiled eggs and shishito peppers into the pot. Simmer a final 8–10 minutes. Lightly crack the eggs with a spoon — this creates fissures that allow the soy sauce to penetrate and produce the classic marbled interior.
8. **Cool and shred.** Remove from heat and allow to cool to room temperature. Shred the beef along the grain (see below). Drizzle braising liquid over everything before serving.

**How to shred with the grain:** Hold the cooled beef with tongs and use two forks to pull fibres apart lengthwise, following the long muscle strands. Pulling *with* the grain produces the characteristic long, chewy flakes of jangjorim. Pulling *against* the grain creates short fragments that turn soft and mushy once stored in sauce — the texture you want to avoid.

## The 2-Minute Butter-Jangjorim Rice Bowl

This is a legendary comfort meal in Korea, and one of the most satisfying things you can do with leftover jangjorim. Take a bowl of piping hot steamed rice, add 1 tbsp of unsalted butter, a generous portion of shredded jangjorim, and 2 tbsp of the chilled braising liquid. Mix until the butter melts and coats every grain. The richness of the dairy against the salty soy is a core flavour memory for almost every Korean child — and it takes two minutes flat.

## Common Substitutions

- **Beef → Pork loin or tenderloin:** Budget-friendly and common in Korean households. Use the same method; reduce simmering time to 30–35 minutes.
- **Chicken eggs → Quail eggs:** Use 12–15; reduce final simmering to 5 minutes.
- **Shishito peppers → Padron, jalapeño, or green chilli:** Jalapeño adds real heat; padron stays mild.
- **Mirin → Dry sherry or apple juice:** Both provide mild sweetness and acidity.
- **Sugar → Honey or coconut sugar:** Honey adds a floral note; coconut sugar gives a rounder sweetness.

## Pro Tips

- **Taste before reducing.** Soy sauces vary significantly in saltiness. Adjust with sugar first — not more soy — to find balance before the final reduction.
- **Don't discard the Stage 1 broth.** The leftover aromatic liquid is a flavour-packed stock. Use it as a base for Korean rice porridge (*jook*) or noodle soups.
- **Make it ahead.** Jangjorim tastes better on day two once the shredded beef has spent overnight absorbing the chilled sauce.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Adding soy sauce too early.** The single most common mistake. Salt causes proteins to contract before they tenderise, and no amount of additional simmering will fix it.
- **Skipping the pre-soak.** The soak removes myoglobin and produces a noticeably cleaner, less gamey final sauce.
- **Over-reducing the sauce.** Jangjorim sauce should coat and gloss the beef, not become a dry crust. Add a tablespoon of water if the sauce looks sticky rather than glossy in the final minutes.
- **Using a thin or lean cut.** Cuts with no intramuscular structure dry out completely during a long braise. Use brisket, shank, or eye round.
- **Storing beef above the sauce line.** Meat exposed to air in the fridge dries into hard flakes. Always keep the beef submerged in braising liquid.

## Easy Variations

**Spicy Jangjorim:** Add 1–2 sliced jalapeños or 1 tsp gochugaru during the soy sauce stage.

**Garlic-forward Jangjorim:** Add 8–10 whole peeled garlic cloves in the final braising step. They turn sweet, jammy, and intensely savoury.

**Mushroom Jangjorim (vegetarian):** Replace beef with 250g torn king oyster mushrooms. Reduce initial simmering to 15 minutes. The mushrooms absorb the sauce beautifully and provide a meaty texture without the meat.

**Pork Jangjorim:** Lean pork loin produces a lighter-coloured, milder version using the same two-stage method.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight glass container with beef fully submerged in sauce. Keeps up to 10–14 days. The soy sauce acts as a natural preservative.

> ⚠️ **Egg safety — important:** While the beef and sauce keep safely for up to two weeks, the eggs are a different matter. **Remove and eat the eggs within 3–4 days.** If you want to extend the batch beyond that, take the eggs out and store them separately, or simply leave them out of the next batch.

**The white layer on top is normal.** When you open the container the next day, you will likely see a layer of solidified white fat on the surface. This is not spoilage — it is rendered beef fat that has firmed up in the cold. Skim it off before serving, or stir it back in when reheating. Both are fine.

**Freezer:** Portion into freezer-safe containers and freeze for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator.

**Reheating:** Jangjorim is traditionally served cold or at room temperature. If you prefer it warm, microwave for 20–30 seconds. Chilled beef firms up — let it rest at room temperature 15–20 minutes for a softer texture before serving.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What cut of beef is best for jangjorim?** Brisket is the top choice for home cooks because its collagen and intramuscular fat produce a moist, flavourful result after a long braise. Eye round is the more traditional cut and yields leaner, finer flakes. Shank and flank steak are excellent middle-ground options. Avoid tender cuts like sirloin or tenderloin — they lack the structural fibres needed for this cooking method and dry out quickly.

**2. Why is my jangjorim beef tough?** Almost certainly because soy sauce was added before the meat had fully tenderised. Salt causes proteins to contract and lock up moisture. Always complete the full 45-minute unseasoned simmer before any soy sauce enters the pot.

**3. Can I make jangjorim in an Instant Pot?** Yes. Pressure cook the beef with aromatics on high for 25 minutes, then quick release. Strain the broth, return everything to sauté mode with soy sauce, mirin, and sugar for 10 minutes. Add eggs and peppers for the final 5 minutes. The texture is slightly more uniform than stovetop, but still very good.

**4. How long does jangjorim last in the fridge?** The beef and sauce keep safely for up to two weeks when stored with the meat submerged in liquid. The eggs must be consumed within 3–4 days and should be stored or removed separately for longer batches.

**5. Is jangjorim eaten hot or cold?** It is traditionally served cold or at room temperature, taken straight from the fridge as part of a banchan spread. It is one of the few Korean side dishes specifically designed to be eaten chilled. Warming it briefly in the microwave is entirely acceptable if preferred.

## Final Note

Jangjorim rewards patience over technique. The steps are simple — soak, simmer, season, reduce — but the sequencing matters more than in most recipes. Get the soy sauce in at the right moment and you'll have tender, glossy, deeply flavoured beef that keeps all week and improves with every passing day. Once you make it once, it becomes the kind of thing you keep permanently stocked in your fridge.
