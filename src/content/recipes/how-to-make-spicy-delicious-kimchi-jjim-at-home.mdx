**Kimchi Jjim** (김치찜) is a Korean braised dish made by slow-cooking aged kimchi with pork belly, gochugaru, and aromatics until the kimchi collapses into a sticky, unctuous, deeply savory braise. It is served as a main dish alongside steamed rice.

 Prep time: 15 minutes. Cook time: 60–90 minutes. Serves 4.

## What Is Kimchi Jjim?

Kimchi Jjim (김치찜) translates roughly to "braised kimchi" in Korean. Unlike [Kimchi Jjigae (Kimchi Stew)](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-kimchi-jjigae-kimchi-stew/), which is brothy and served as a soup course, Kimchi Jjim uses very little liquid. The kimchi and pork braise low and slow until the kimchi collapses completely, its sourness mellows, and the rendered pork fat absorbs into every layer — creating something melt-in-your-mouth tender and intensely rich.

The dish is especially prized when made with **mukeunji** (묵은지) — super-aged kimchi fermented for six months to a year or more — which Korean home cooks traditionally set aside specifically for braising. Mukeunji is tangier, softer, and far more complex than regular kimchi, and it transforms this dish into something extraordinary. [Kimchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kimchi), the fermented cabbage at its heart, is central to Korean cuisine and is classified as a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage.

## Why This Recipe Works

The key is **aged kimchi** and **low, slow heat**. Young kimchi stays sharp and acidic. Kimchi fermented for 3–6 weeks softens beautifully and develops the deep umami funk this dish needs. Mukeunji, if available, takes it even further.

### Recipe Testing Notes

**Batch 1 — Fresh kimchi, high heat:** The kimchi stayed crunchy and tasted raw. The pork was chewy. Abandoned after 45 minutes.

**Batch 2 — Aged kimchi (4 weeks), high heat:** Better flavor but the kimchi broke down unevenly and the liquid reduced too fast, causing scorching on the base.

**Batch 3 — Aged kimchi, low heat, 90 minutes, no lid:** The kimchi melted beautifully but the dish dried out. Added ½ cup water at 45 minutes. Good results but slightly bland.

**Batch 4 — Aged kimchi, fish sauce added at 30 minutes, covered for first 45 min then uncovered:** This was the breakthrough. Fish sauce deepened the umami without making it salty. The two-stage cooking — covered first to build moisture, then uncovered to caramelize — created the glossy, sticky finish that defines a great Kimchi Jjim. **Optimal result.**

**Batch 5 — Substituted gochujang for gochugaru:** Muddier, sweeter flavor. Gochugaru keeps the colour vivid and the heat clean. Gochujang works but is not preferred.

**Failure documented:** Using water-rinsed kimchi to reduce sourness produced a flat, lifeless dish. Never rinse your kimchi for this recipe — the fermentation brine is the backbone of the flavour.

## **Kimchi Jjim** (김치찜) ngredients

- 400g (14 oz) aged kimchi, roughly chopped (3–6 weeks fermented; mukeunji if available)
- 300g (10.5 oz) pork belly, cut into 2-inch pieces
- 3 tbsp kimchi juice (from the kimchi jar)
- 1 tbsp gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes)
- 1 tbsp fish sauce
- 1 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tsp sugar
- 4 garlic cloves, minced
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- ½ cup water or anchovy stock
- 2 spring onions, sliced (for garnish)
- 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds (for garnish)

## Equipment

- **Heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven** — Essential. Cast iron or enameled cast iron retains heat evenly and prevents scorching during the long braise. A thin pot will burn the kimchi. *Testing confirmed the Dutch oven produced the most consistent results across all batches.*
- **Tongs** — For turning pork belly pieces without piercing them and releasing the rendered fat prematurely.
- **Sharp knife and cutting board** — Pork belly and kimchi both need firm, confident cuts.
- **Ladle** — For checking the liquid level and redistributing the braise every 15 minutes.
- **Lid** — Required for the first stage of braising to trap steam and build moisture before the uncovered reduction phase.

## How to Make Kimchi Jjim (Step-by-Step)

1. **Sear the pork belly.** Heat your Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Add pork belly pieces in a single layer (no oil needed — the fat renders naturally). Sear for 2–3 minutes per side until lightly browned. Do not crowd the pan. The fat rendering here is not waste — it becomes the sauce.
2. **Add aromatics.** Reduce heat to medium. Add minced garlic and grated ginger directly to the rendered pork fat. Stir for 30 seconds until fragrant.
3. **Add kimchi and seasonings.** Add the chopped kimchi, kimchi juice, gochugaru, soy sauce, fish sauce, and sugar. Stir to combine and coat the pork evenly.
4. **Add liquid and cover.** Pour in ½ cup water or anchovy stock. Cover with a lid and reduce heat to low. Braise for 45 minutes, checking every 15 minutes to ensure there is still liquid at the bottom.
5. **Uncover and reduce.** Remove the lid. Continue braising over low heat for a further 30–45 minutes. The liquid will reduce, the kimchi will deepen in colour, and the pork fat will turn unctuous and glossy — coating every piece of kimchi like a sticky glaze.
6. **Finish and serve.** Drizzle sesame oil over the dish. Taste and adjust seasoning. Garnish with sliced spring onions and sesame seeds. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

## Common Substitutions

- **Pork belly → Pork shoulder:** Leaner but still works. Reduce cook time by 15 minutes to avoid dryness.
- **Pork → Tuna (canned):** A classic Korean pantry version. Add tuna in the last 10 minutes only.
- **Fish sauce → Soy sauce:** Works, but you lose depth. Add an extra ½ tsp sugar to compensate.
- **Anchovy stock → Water:** Fine. Anchovy stock adds another umami layer but is not essential.
- **Gochugaru → Gochujang:** Use half the quantity. The dish will be slightly sweeter and less vivid in colour.

## Pro Tips

- **Use the oldest kimchi you have.** Kimchi under 2 weeks old will not reach this dish's potential. If you can source mukeunji (6 months+), use it without hesitation.
- **Sugar is a chemical balancer, not a sweetener.** Very old kimchi accumulates significant lactic acid during fermentation. The 1 tsp of sugar in this recipe doesn't make the dish sweet — it chemically counteracts that sharp acidity and rounds out the overall flavour. If your kimchi is extremely sour, increase sugar to 1½ tsp.
- **Don't skip the kimchi juice.** It acts as a concentrated seasoning base and adds fermented depth that plain water cannot replicate.
- **Deglaze if it sticks.** A small splash of water and a wooden spoon will lift any caramelized bits — these are flavour, not a problem.
- **Rest before serving.** Kimchi Jjim tastes better after 10 minutes off the heat, and is exceptional reheated the next day once the flavours have fully merged.
- **Pro Aesthetic Tip:** For a restaurant-style presentation, braise the kimchi as whole or quarter-leaf pieces rather than chopped. Lay the pork belly on top of the whole leaves and cook them intact. At the table, snip everything into pieces with kitchen scissors. The leaves hold their structure longer, the dish looks dramatic, and it is the traditional way to serve it.

## Sourness Correction Guide

Kimchi acidity varies wildly depending on brand and age. If the flavour needs adjusting mid-cook, use this quick reference:


| If the braise is... | Do this                                                                                                |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Too sour            | Add ½ tsp more sugar or a splash of mirin                                                              |
| Too salty           | Add 2 tbsp water and a thick slice of raw potato to absorb excess salt                                 |
| Too bitter          | Kimchi may be extremely old; add a small knob of butter or an extra drizzle of sesame oil to mellow it |

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using fresh kimchi.** It stays sharp and crunchy and the dish tastes unfinished.
- **Cooking on high heat.** The kimchi scorches and the pork tightens. Low and slow is non-negotiable.
- **Adding too much water.** This turns it into a soup rather than a braise. Less liquid, more patience.
- **Skipping the sear on the pork.** Without browning, the fat renders poorly and the dish lacks depth.
- **Rinsing the kimchi before cooking.** This destroys the fermentation brine — the entire soul of this dish.

## Easy Variations

- **Kimchi Jjim with Tofu:** Add firm tofu cut into 1-inch cubes in the final 15 minutes. It absorbs the braising liquid and becomes silky.
- **Spicy Version:** Double the gochugaru and add 1 tsp gochujang for an extra layer of heat and body.
- **Anchovy Kimchi Jjim:** Replace pork with whole dried anchovies for a lighter, intensely savoury result common in coastal Korean cooking.
- **Vegetarian Kimchi Jjim:** Use vegan kimchi (no shrimp paste), replace pork with king oyster mushrooms, and swap fish sauce for soy sauce. The mushrooms braise beautifully and develop a genuinely meaty texture.

## Serving Suggestions

Kimchi Jjim is best served family-style at the center of the table. The sticky, reduced braising liquid is specifically meant to be spooned directly over rice.

- **Steamed short-grain white rice** — The classic and non-negotiable pairing.
- **Purple rice (heukmi-bap) or multigrain rice (japgok-bap)** — Traditional Korean home-cooked alternatives that add a nutty, earthy depth and pair beautifully with the intensity of the braise.
- **Simple banchan** — A crisp [Korean cucumber kimchi](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/quick-korean-cucumber-kimchi/) offers cool, crunchy contrast to the rich, sticky pork.
- **Gyeran-jjim (Korean steamed eggs)** — Their mild, custardy texture balances the assertive flavours of the jjim.
- **Cold barley tea (boricha)** — A traditional Korean table drink that cuts cleanly through the rendered fat.

## Storage and Reheating

- **Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The flavour deepens overnight — the next day is arguably better.
- **Freezer:** Freeze for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before reheating.
- **Reheating:** Add 2–3 tbsp water to the pot and reheat covered over low heat for 8–10 minutes. Avoid microwave reheating — it makes the pork rubbery and the kimchi loses its texture.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What is the difference between Kimchi Jjim and Kimchi Jjigae?** Kimchi Jjigae is a brothy soup with a significant liquid base, often eaten as a standalone soup course. Kimchi Jjim is a dry braise — very little liquid remains at the end, and the kimchi becomes sticky and caramelized. They share ingredients but produce entirely different textures and eating experiences.

**2. What is mukeunji and do I need it for this recipe?** Mukeunji (묵은지) is kimchi that has been aged for six months to a year or more. It is tangier, softer, and significantly more complex than regular kimchi, and Korean cooks traditionally use it for braised dishes like Kimchi Jjim. You don't need it — 3–6 week aged kimchi delivers excellent results — but if you can find it at a Korean grocery, it elevates the dish noticeably.

**3. Can I make Kimchi Jjim without pork?** Yes. Canned tuna is a very common Korean home-cooking substitute — add it in the final 10 minutes only. Firm tofu and king oyster mushrooms are both excellent meat-free alternatives that absorb the braising liquid beautifully.

**4. How spicy is Kimchi Jjim?** Moderate heat by default, depending on your kimchi. Reduce gochugaru to ½ tbsp for milder heat, or omit it and rely solely on the kimchi's natural spice. To increase heat, double the gochugaru and add a small spoonful of gochujang.

**5. Why is my Kimchi Jjim dry or burnt at the bottom?** Too-high heat or too little liquid. Fix it by reducing heat to the absolute lowest setting and adding small water splashes (2–3 tbsp at a time) whenever the base looks dry. The dish should always have a thin film of liquid throughout the braise, especially in the first 45 minutes.

## Final Note

Kimchi Jjim rewards patience and the willingness to use the most forgotten jar at the back of your fridge — the very old, very sour kimchi that most people skip over. Give it low heat, give it time, and trust the fermentation. The pork fat renders into the kimchi, the lactic acid mellows, and the whole dish becomes something far greater than the sum of its parts. If you can get your hands on mukeunji, use it. You will not be disappointed.
