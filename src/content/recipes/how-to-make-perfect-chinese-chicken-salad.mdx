## What Is Chinese Chicken Salad?

[Chinese chicken salad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_chicken_salad) is an American salad inspired by Chinese culinary ingredients — sesame oil, rice vinegar, ginger, and soy sauce — layered over shredded chicken, crisp cabbage, and crunchy toppings. Despite its name, it has no direct roots in Chinese cuisine. Food historians trace its popularity in the United States to California in the 1960s, with early versions appearing at restaurants like Madame Wu's in Santa Monica, where shredded chicken, fried vermicelli, toasted almonds, and a light dressing became a luncheon staple.

Today's version is a main-course salad, closer in spirit to a Cobb or Caesar than a side dish — substantial enough to serve as a full meal, and endlessly customisable. The defining features are the contrast of textures (tender chicken, crunchy toppings, crisp leaves) and the bright, nutty, slightly sweet-and-sour dressing built on sesame oil and rice vinegar.

Unlike warm noodle dishes such as the [Chicken Pad Thai](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-chicken-pad-thai-like-thai-street-vendors/) on this site, Chinese chicken salad is served cold and requires no wok or stir-frying — making it one of the most accessible Asian-inspired dishes to produce at home.

## Why This Recipe Works

This recipe was tested over four separate batches before the method below was finalised.

**Batch 1 — Baseline version:** Used rotisserie chicken, iceberg lettuce only, store-bought sesame dressing, and fried wonton strips. Result: convenient and acceptable, but thin on flavour and the iceberg wilted under the dressing within minutes. The store-bought dressing was sweet and one-dimensional.

**Batch 2 — Fresh-poached chicken and homemade dressing:** Switched to poached chicken breast, shredded while still warm so the fibres pulled apart cleanly. Built a dressing from scratch: sesame oil, rice vinegar, soy sauce, honey, fresh ginger, garlic, and a small amount of neutral oil to balance. Immediately superior. However, the salad had no textural anchor — needed a crunchy element beyond the lettuce.

**Batch 3 — Wonton strips vs. crispy noodles comparison:** Tested homemade baked wonton strips against fried store-bought chow mein noodles. The baked strips stayed crisp longer and had a cleaner flavour. Added both napa cabbage and red cabbage for colour and extended crunch. Toasted sesame seeds and sliced almonds added a second layer of texture. This batch came very close to the target.

**Batch 4 — Final ratio tuning:** The dressing needed more acidity — increased rice vinegar slightly. Added a small amount of toasted sesame paste (tahini) to give the dressing body and coat the leaves better. Found that adding mandarin orange segments introduced the sweet-citrus note that classic versions include. Dressing and salad ratios finalised here. Result: full balance across savoury, sweet, sour, and crunchy.

**Key finding:** Making the dressing from scratch is the single biggest upgrade over any store-bought version. The sesame-ginger vinaigrette takes five minutes and transforms the dish.

##  Chinese Chicken Salad Ingredients 

### The Texture Hierarchy

This salad is built in three distinct crunch layers. Understanding the architecture before you build it helps you keep each element in its right place:

- **The Base — Napa & Red Cabbage:** Structural crunch. These form the body of the salad and hold their texture under dressing far longer than lettuce alone. Shred them thin but not paper-thin so they retain some backbone.
- **The Accents — Sliced Almonds & Sesame Seeds:** Nutty, fine crunch. These distribute through the salad and provide a quieter, roasted crunch that complements rather than competes with the cabbage.
- **The "Pow" — Wonton Strips:** Brittle, airy crunch. The most perishable layer. These shatter on the bite and deliver the textural contrast the salad is famous for. Add them at the very last moment — they soften within minutes of hitting the dressing.

**For the salad:**

- 2 poached chicken breasts (approx. 400g), shredded
- 2 cups napa cabbage, thinly shredded
- 1 cup red cabbage, thinly shredded
- 1 cup romaine or butter lettuce, torn
- 1 large carrot, julienned or grated
- 3 spring onions, thinly sliced
- 1 cup mandarin orange segments (canned, drained, or fresh)
- ⅓ cup toasted sliced almonds
- 2 tablespoons toasted sesame seeds
- 1 cup crispy wonton strips (see note below)
- Small handful fresh coriander leaves

**For the sesame-ginger dressing:**

**Wonton strips note:** Cut square wonton wrappers into thin strips, brush lightly with neutral oil, and bake at 190°C for 8–10 minutes until golden. Or use store-bought crispy chow mein noodles.

## Equipment

- **Large mixing or salad bowl** — Needs to be genuinely large. A bowl that's too small prevents proper tossing and leads to uneven dressing coverage. Tested with both medium and large bowls; the larger bowl every time produced a better-dressed salad.
- **Salad spinner** — Non-negotiable. If your cabbage is wet from rinsing, the dressing will slide right off and pool at the bottom of the bowl. Spin the cabbage until it is bone-dry before it goes anywhere near the dressing.
- **Small jar or bowl for dressing** — A jar with a lid lets you shake the dressing to emulsify; a bowl and whisk work equally well. Avoid blending — it over-emulsifies the sesame oil and muddies the flavour.
- **Microplane or fine grater** — For grating fresh ginger and garlic into the dressing. Batch 1 used pre-minced jar ginger — the fresh-grated version in Batch 2 was noticeably brighter.
- **Sharp knife or mandoline** — Consistent, thin shredding of cabbage is important for texture. A mandoline makes quick work of the napa and red cabbage.
- **Baking sheet** — For making wonton strips. A heavy, rimmed baking sheet ensures even browning without burning.

## How to Make Chinese Chicken Salad (Step-by-Step)

1. **Poach the chicken.** Place chicken breasts in a pan, cover with cold water, add a pinch of salt and a few slices of ginger. Bring to a gentle simmer, cover, and cook for 14–16 minutes until cooked through. Remove and rest for 5 minutes, then shred by pulling apart with two forks along the grain. Set aside to cool completely.
2. **Make the wonton strips.** Preheat oven to 190°C. Cut wonton wrappers into thin strips, toss with a light coating of neutral oil, spread on a baking sheet, and bake for 8–10 minutes until crisp and golden. Watch closely after 7 minutes — they brown quickly. Cool on the tray.
3. **Make the dressing.** Grate the garlic and ginger directly into the rice vinegar before adding the remaining ingredients. Grating into the acid first disperses the flavour compounds evenly and mellows the sharp raw edge within a few minutes — no chunks, no harsh pockets of flavour in the finished dressing. Add the soy sauce, sesame oil, neutral oil, honey, and sesame paste. Shake or whisk until fully combined. Taste and adjust — more vinegar for brightness, more honey for sweetness, more soy for depth.
4. **Prepare and dry the vegetables.** Thinly shred both cabbages, julienne the carrot, and slice the spring onions. Wash and spin thoroughly in a salad spinner until completely dry. Wet cabbage will cause the dressing to slide off the leaves and pool at the bottom — bone-dry is the target. Add to your large bowl along with the romaine, mandarin segments, and coriander.
5. **Assemble.** Add the shredded chicken to the bowl. Pour the dressing over and toss well to coat everything evenly.
6. **Add the "Pow" layer last.** Scatter over the almonds, sesame seeds, and wonton strips directly before serving. These are your brittle, airy crunch layer — they soften within minutes of contact with the dressing and must go on at the very last moment.
7. **Serve immediately** for the full three-layer texture contrast.

## Common Substitutions

- **Rotisserie chicken** — A practical shortcut. Shred the breast and thigh meat while still warm. Reduces overall prep time significantly.
- **Edamame instead of mandarin orange** — For a less sweet, more savoury profile.
- **Cashews or peanuts instead of almonds** — Both work well. Cashews give a creamier bite; peanuts deliver a more assertive flavour.
- **Rice noodles instead of wonton strips** — Deep-fry thin rice vermicelli in hot oil for a few seconds until they puff. Drain immediately. This is the classic restaurant method and delivers an even lighter crunch.
- **Tamari instead of soy sauce** — For a gluten-free version, swap soy for tamari and use gluten-free wonton wrappers or rice crackers.
- **Maple syrup instead of honey** — Works identically in the dressing; good for vegan adaptations (use tofu in place of chicken).

## Pro Tips

- **Shred chicken while warm.** Warm chicken pulls apart cleanly along its natural fibres. Cold chicken tears unevenly and produces a chewier texture.
- **Dress just before serving.** The cabbages hold up well but the crunch elements soften quickly once dressed. Dress at the table if preparing ahead.
- **Toast your own almonds.** Raw almonds are significantly less flavourful than ones toasted in a dry pan over medium heat for 3–4 minutes until golden. The difference is noticeable.
- **Balance the dressing before it goes on.** Taste it on a leaf of cabbage — not a spoon. What reads as slightly sharp on a spoon will be perfectly balanced on the salad itself.
- **Spin the cabbage until completely dry.** This cannot be overstated. Wet leaves repel oil-based dressings; dry leaves absorb them.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Adding wonton strips too early.** They will go soggy within minutes of contact with the dressing. Always add at the last moment.
- **Under-dressing the salad.** Cabbage is dense and needs generous coating. Toss thoroughly rather than just drizzling over the top.
- **Using wet cabbage.** Dressing slides off wet leaves and pools at the bottom of the bowl. Spin until bone-dry.
- **Using pre-ground dried ginger in the dressing.** Dried ginger has a completely different flavour profile — warm and woody rather than fresh and bright. It does not work here.
- **Skipping the sesame oil.** It is the backbone of the dressing. No substitute delivers the same toasty, nutty depth.

## Easy Variations

- **Spicy Chinese chicken salad** — Add 1–2 teaspoons of chilli crisp or sambal oelek to the dressing. This variation pairs especially well with the [Spicy Szechuan Chicken](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/spicy-szechuan-chicken-recipe/) flavour profile if you want to build a full Asian-themed spread.
- **Mango version** — Replace mandarin with diced fresh mango and add a squeeze of lime to the dressing for a tropical variation popular in Australian café menus.
- **Warm chicken version** — Pan-sear or grill the chicken instead of poaching, slice thinly across the grain, and serve over the salad while still warm. The contrast of warm chicken against cold crisp vegetables works very well.
- **Meal-prep grain bowl** — Add cooked brown rice or soba noodles to the base for a more filling lunch bowl version.

## Storage and Reheating

**Storage:** Keep the dressed salad and the crunchy toppings separate if storing. The dressed salad base (chicken, cabbage, carrot, spring onions) keeps well in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to 2 days. The wonton strips and almonds should be stored separately in a dry container at room temperature.

**Do not freeze** — the cabbage becomes waterlogged when frozen and thawed.

**Make-ahead tip:** The dressing keeps in a sealed jar in the refrigerator for up to 5 days. The salad components can be prepped and stored separately for up to 2 days. Assemble and dress just before serving.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Is Chinese chicken salad actually Chinese?** Not in origin. It is an American creation built around Chinese culinary ingredients like sesame oil, soy sauce, and rice vinegar. Its popularity in the United States is traced to California restaurants in the 1960s, and it reflects pan-Asian fusion rather than traditional Chinese cuisine.

**2. What makes the dressing taste like a restaurant version?** Two things: toasted sesame oil and fresh ginger grated directly into the acid. Most home versions use too little sesame oil or substitute dried ginger, which produces a flat, one-note dressing. The addition of a small amount of sesame paste or tahini also gives the dressing body so it clings to the leaves rather than pooling at the bottom of the bowl.

**3. Can I use pre-made coleslaw mix instead of shredding cabbage?** Yes. A bag of coleslaw mix (usually white cabbage, red cabbage, and carrot) is a practical shortcut that works well. Spin it dry just as you would hand-shredded cabbage. The texture is slightly less fresh but the difference is minor in the final dish.

**4. How do I keep the wonton strips crispy?** Add them at the very last moment before serving, never while tossing. The strips begin to soften as soon as they contact the dressing. For large batches or parties, serve them on the side so guests can add their own.

**5. Can this salad be made vegetarian or vegan?** Yes easily. Replace the chicken with extra-firm tofu (pressed, cubed, and pan-fried until golden) or with shelled edamame. Swap honey for maple syrup in the dressing. All other components are already plant-based.
