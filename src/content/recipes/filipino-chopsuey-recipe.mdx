Filipino Chopsuey is a colorful, protein-packed stir-fry of mixed vegetables — cabbage, carrots, cauliflower, snap peas, and bell peppers — cooked with pork, chicken, shrimp, or a combination of all three in a savory oyster-and-soy sauce glaze. It comes together in under 30 minutes and is one of the most versatile everyday meals in Filipino home cooking. The hallmark is a thick, translucent, oyster-sauce glaze that clings to every vegetable — the signature look of a proper *fiesta* Chopsuey.

## What Is Filipino Chopsuey?

Filipino Chopsuey (also spelled *chop suey*) is the Philippines' adaptation of a dish that traces its roots to Chinese-American immigrant cooking. As noted on [Wikipedia's entry on chop suey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chop_suey), the original dish was built around meat, eggs, and vegetables in a starch-thickened sauce — concepts that Filipino cooks have made entirely their own over generations.

The Filipino version is heartier and more intensely flavored than its Chinese-American counterpart. It leans heavily on oyster sauce, fish sauce (*patis*), and a cornstarch slurry to create that signature glossy coating. Some households add hard-boiled quail eggs or a small amount of pork liver — touches that signal a celebratory preparation. It is a *fiesta* staple and a weeknight regular, the kind of dish every Filipino household makes slightly differently.

### Recipe Testing Notes

Getting Filipino Chopsuey right took multiple rounds of testing to nail down timing, sauce balance, vegetable texture, and liquid control.

**Batch 1 — The soggy failure.** All vegetables were added at once with the sauce. Result: overcooked, waterlogged vegetables with a diluted sauce that pooled at the bottom of the pan. Identified problem: different vegetables need staggered cooking times.

**Batch 2 — Staggered approach, wrong order.** Leafy cabbage went in first, denser carrots and cauliflower second. Result: mush on the cabbage, undercooked carrots. Key lesson: **density — not visual size — dictates cook order.** A thin carrot slice takes longer than a large cabbage chunk because cellular density controls how quickly heat penetrates.

**Batch 3 — Correct staging, sauce too thin.** Dense vegetables first, aromatics early, leafy last. Sauce remained watery — the blanched vegetables were not dried before entering the wok. Residual surface moisture, combined with the ½ cup of broth, created a soup rather than a glaze. Tested 1 tsp cornstarch vs 2 tsp: 2 tsp produced the right clingy glaze. An ice bath followed by thorough patting dry solved the liquid problem entirely.

**Batch 4 — Optimal result.** High heat throughout, vegetables blanched then shocked in iced water, thoroughly dried before stir-frying, 2:1 ratio of oyster sauce to soy sauce. Vegetables retained a firm bite, sauce clung evenly, colors stayed vibrant. Quail eggs added at the end held their shape perfectly. Adding 1 tablespoon of canned liver spread to the sauce base produced a noticeably more complex glaze.

**Alternatives tried:** Water chestnuts added pleasant crunch but pulled focus from the sauce. Baby corn worked well. Hoisin substituted for oyster sauce was too sweet. Oyster sauce is non-negotiable for authenticity.

## Filipino Chopsuey Ingredients

![ Raw Filipino Chopsuey ingredients arranged in ceramic prep bowls on dark slate surface](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Filipino-Chopsuey-b-1536.webp)

**For the stir-fry:**

- 200g pork shoulder or chicken breast, thinly sliced
- 150g medium shrimp, peeled and deveined
- 2 cups cabbage, cut into 2-inch pieces
- 1 cup cauliflower florets
- 1 medium carrot, julienned
- 1 cup snap peas or snow peas, strings removed
- 1 red bell pepper, sliced into strips
- 1 cup young corn (baby corn), halved
- 8–10 hard-boiled quail eggs, peeled *(optional but traditional for special-occasion versions)*
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 medium onion, sliced
- 2 tablespoons cooking oil

**For the sauce:**

- 3 tablespoons oyster sauce
- 1½ tablespoons soy sauce
- 1 teaspoon fish sauce (*patis*) — start with ½ tsp; see *patis* warning in Pro Tips
- ½ teaspoon sugar
- ½ cup chicken broth or water
- 2 teaspoons cornstarch dissolved in 2 tablespoons water
- 1 tablespoon canned liver spread *(optional, for a richer, more traditional sauce depth)*
- Salt and white pepper to taste

## Equipment

Testing confirmed that equipment choices meaningfully affect the final dish.

- **Large wok or wide skillet (14-inch minimum):** A wok is ideal because its sloping sides allow vegetables to toss freely and develop slight char. A flat skillet works but will steam more than stir-fry at high heat. Cast iron retains heat well but is harder to control; carbon steel woks were the clear winner in testing.
- **High-heat burner:** Stir-frying at medium heat produces steamed vegetables. Use the highest heat your burner allows.
- **Sharp chef's knife:** Uniform cuts are the single biggest factor in even cooking. Uneven slices mean half-raw, half-mushy vegetables in the same batch.
- **Bowl of iced water:** Essential for the blanch-and-shock step. Have it ready and filled before the blanching water even comes to a boil — there is no time to fill it mid-cook.
- **Small mixing bowl for slurry:** Pre-mixing the cornstarch slurry before you start cooking prevents clumping when it hits the hot pan.

## How to Make Filipino Chopsuey (Step-by-Step)

![Glazed quail eggs nestled among stir-fried vegetables in Filipino Chopsuey](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Filipino-Chopsuey-e-1536.webp)

1. **Marinate the meat.** Combine sliced pork or chicken with 1 tablespoon soy sauce and a pinch of white pepper. Set aside for 10 minutes.
2. **Make the sauce.** In a small bowl, whisk together oyster sauce, soy sauce, fish sauce, sugar, and chicken broth. If using liver spread, whisk it in now until completely smooth — no lumps. Set aside. Mix cornstarch with 2 tablespoons of water separately.
3. **Blanch and shock the hard vegetables.** Bring a pot of water to a boil. Add cauliflower and carrots for exactly 1 minute, then immediately lift them out and plunge them into the bowl of iced water. This shocks them, stopping the cooking instantly and locking in their vibrant orange and white color while keeping them snappy for the final stir-fry. Once fully cooled, drain and pat thoroughly dry with a clean kitchen towel. Surface moisture is the primary enemy of a glossy, clingy sauce.
  ![](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Filipino-Chopsuey-d-1536.webp)
4. **Sear the meat.** Heat oil in your wok over high heat until lightly smoking. Add pork or chicken in a single layer. Cook undisturbed for 90 seconds, then toss. Remove from the pan once cooked through. Add shrimp if using, cook for 1–2 minutes until pink, then remove and set aside.
5. **Build the aromatics.** In the same wok, add a splash more oil if needed. Add onion and stir-fry for 1 minute. Add garlic and cook for 30 seconds until fragrant.
6. **Cook vegetables in stages.** Add blanched carrots and cauliflower first, tossing for 2 minutes. Add snap peas and baby corn next, cook for 1 minute. Add bell pepper and cabbage last, cook for another 1–2 minutes. All vegetables should be tender-crisp throughout.
7. **Combine everything.** Return the meat and shrimp to the wok. Pour the sauce over everything and toss to coat. Let it come to a full simmer.
8. **Thicken the sauce.** Give the cornstarch slurry a quick stir and drizzle it in while tossing continuously. The sauce will thicken and turn glossy within 30–60 seconds. This is starch gelatinization in action: when the slurry contacts simmering liquid at approximately 62°C to 70°C (144°F to 158°F), the starch granules swell and burst, trapping water molecules and converting thin broth into a viscous, light-reflecting glaze — the precise moment the dish takes on its *fiesta* sheen. Season with salt and white pepper.
9. **Add quail eggs (if using).** Gently fold in the peeled hard-boiled quail eggs in the final 30 seconds. They only need time to heat through and absorb a thin coat of glaze — do not overcook or they will become rubbery and discolor.
10. **Serve immediately** over steamed white rice.

## Common Substitutions

- **No shrimp:** Use firm tofu, sliced squid, or simply double the pork.
- **No oyster sauce:** A mixture of hoisin sauce and a splash of soy sauce is the closest alternative, though flavor will lean sweeter.
- **Gluten-free:** Use tamari in place of soy sauce and a certified gluten-free oyster sauce.
- **No fish sauce:** A small pinch of salt and a few drops of Worcestershire deepens the savory baseline similarly, though with less complexity.
- **No quail eggs:** Regular hard-boiled chicken eggs, halved, work for a home-style version. Less elegant but entirely acceptable.
- **Vegetable swaps:** Broccoli, bok choy, zucchini, or mushrooms all work well. Keep the density-staging rule — denser vegetables always go in first.

## Pro Tips

- **Blanch, shock, then dry completely.** The ice bath stops overcooking and locks in color. Patting dry is equally critical — residual moisture overwhelms the cornstarch slurry regardless of how much you use.
- **The *patis* warning.** Fish sauce is far more potent than soy sauce and carries a fermented depth that soy sauce cannot replicate. Add it ½ teaspoon at a time — too much will overpower the delicate sweetness of the snap peas and baby corn.
- **Do not crowd the pan.** If your wok is smaller than 14 inches, stir-fry the vegetables in two batches.
- **High heat the whole way.** Lowering the heat shifts cooking from stir-frying to steaming and produces watery results.
- **Add the slurry last.** Cornstarch breaks down under prolonged heat. Add it only once all other ingredients are combined at a full simmer.
- **Taste before serving.** Oyster sauce saltiness varies by brand. Adjust with a pinch of sugar or a final drop of fish sauce before plating.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Skipping the blanch-and-shock and adding all vegetables raw simultaneously — leads to uneven doneness, dull colors, and excess liquid.
- Blanching but not drying the vegetables — dilutes the sauce regardless of how much cornstarch is used.
- Using too much cornstarch — turns the glaze into a gluey paste rather than the translucent coating that defines the dish.
- Cooking at medium heat — steams the vegetables and prevents any caramelization.
- Adding the sauce before vegetables are nearly done — the broth steams rather than coats.
- Overcrowding the wok — drops the pan temperature instantly.

## Easy Variations

**Fiesta-style with quail eggs (traditional):** Add 8–10 hard-boiled, peeled quail eggs in the final 30 seconds. Their creamy interiors contrast the crisp vegetables, and they absorb the glaze on their surface in a way that chicken eggs cannot match. Their presence immediately signals a celebratory, *handaan*-grade preparation.

**With liver spread for depth:** Whisk 1 tablespoon of canned liver spread directly into your sauce base before cooking. It adds the savory, earthy richness of traditional pork or chicken liver without any textural resistance — the approach commonly used in high-end Filipino catering. All the authentic flavor, none of the polarizing texture.

**Chopsuey Guisado:** Add 2 tablespoons of tomato sauce to the base for a slightly tangy sauce popular in Visayan households.

**All-vegetable Chopsuey:** Skip the meat and shrimp. Use vegetable broth and season generously with patis and a dash of sesame oil. ## Storage and Reheating

![Filipino Chopsuey stored in a matte ceramic lidded container for meal prep](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Filipino-Chopsuey-g-1536.webp)

- **Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 3 days. Vegetables will soften slightly but flavors deepen overnight.
- **Freezer:** Not recommended. Most vegetables lose their texture completely, and the cornstarch sauce breaks and turns grainy on reheating.
- **Reheating:** Reheat in a wok over medium-high heat with a tablespoon of broth to loosen the sauce. Avoid the microwave — it steams vegetables unevenly and causes the sauce to separate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What is the difference between Filipino Chopsuey and Chinese chop suey?** Filipino Chopsuey uses oyster sauce and fish sauce (*patis*) as its flavor base, producing a deeper umami profile. Chinese-American chop suey is lighter, relies heavily on bean sprouts, and is seasoned primarily with soy sauce. Filipino versions include a broader vegetable variety, combine multiple proteins, and often feature quail eggs and liver — ingredients absent from the Chinese-American version.

**2. Can I make Filipino Chopsuey ahead of time?** You can blanch and shock the vegetables, marinate the meat, and mix the sauce base up to 24 hours in advance, storing each component separately. However, stir-fry and finish the dish just before serving. Pre-cooked Chopsuey reheats reasonably well but vegetables lose crispness and the sauce can thin out.

**3. Why does my Chopsuey always come out watery?** Three compounding causes: not blanching dense vegetables first (they expel water during stir-frying), failing to dry them after blanching, and cooking at too low a temperature. Residual moisture from insufficiently dried vegetables alone can overwhelm the cornstarch slurry. Fix all three and the sauce will be glossy and clingy.

**4. What makes quail eggs special in Filipino Chopsuey?** Quail eggs are proportionally richer in yolk and have a firmer white than chicken eggs, allowing them to hold their shape intact in a hot stir-fry. They absorb the oyster-sauce glaze on their surface and signal a *fiesta*-grade preparation. Their presence immediately elevates a weeknight Chopsuey into something appropriate for celebrations and catered events.

**5. Is Filipino Chopsuey healthy?** Yes. It delivers substantial protein alongside a broad range of vegetables providing fiber, vitamins C and K, and minerals. The main consideration is sodium from the oyster and soy sauces. Switching to low-sodium soy sauce and using patis carefully — starting at ½ teaspoon — brings sodium to a moderate level without altering the dish's character meaningfully.

## Final Note

Filipino Chopsuey rewards technique far more than ingredient quantity. The blanch-and-shock step, drying the vegetables before the wok, staging by density rather than visual size, and adding the cornstarch slurry at precisely the right moment — these four moves separate a vibrant, restaurant-grade result from a watery, muted one. Add quail eggs and a spoonful of liver spread when you want proper *fiesta* territory. Master those fundamentals and this becomes one of the most reliable, adaptable stir-fries in your Filipino cooking repertoire.
