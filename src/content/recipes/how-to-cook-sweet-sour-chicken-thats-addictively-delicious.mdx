**Sweet and Sour Chicken** is a classic Chinese-American dish featuring bite-sized crispy fried chicken tossed in a vibrant sauce made from vinegar, sugar, and ketchup. Success depends on velveting the meat with cornstarch and achieving a "glass-like" crunch through double-frying or high-heat searing

This guide masters the dual secrets of **velveting** and **double-frying** to deliver a vibrant, high-viscosity glaze and a texture that actually snaps. ## **What Is  Sweet and Sour Chicken**

Sweet and Sour Chicken (甜酸鸡, *tián suān jī*) is a global culinary staple that evolved from traditional Cantonese sweet and sour pork. While the authentic Cantonese version focuses on a delicate balance of hawthorn and vinegar, the modern Americanized version utilizes ketchup and pineapple juice to create a bolder, more nostalgic flavor profile. It is characterized by the "agrodolce" (sweet-and-sour) interplay and the textural contrast between the sauce-coated crust and the juicy meat.

## **Ingredients You’ll Need**

To ensure semantic depth and flavor complexity, organize your mise en place into three categories:

### **1. The Chicken & Marinade (Velveting)**

- **1.5 lbs Chicken Thighs:** Preferred over breast for higher fat content and moisture retention.
- **1 tbsp Soy Sauce:** For umami and salt.
- **1 tbsp Shaoxing Wine:** (Optional) For depth of aroma.
- **1/4 tsp Baking Soda:** A chemical tenderizer that breaks down muscle fibers.
- **1/2 cup Cornstarch:** The primary coating for a gluten-free, superior crunch.

![](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Sweet-&-Sour-Chicken-1-1536.webp)

### **2. The Signature Sauce**

- **1/2 cup White Sugar:** The sweet base.
- **1/3 cup Rice Vinegar:** Provides the necessary "sour" lift without the harshness of white vinegar.
- **1/4 cup Ketchup:** Acts as a thickener and provides the iconic red hue.
- **1 tbsp Soy Sauce:** For color and salt balance.
- **1/2 cup Pineapple Juice:** (From the can) For enzymatic sweetness and fruitiness.

### **3. The Aromatics & Vegetables**

- **1 cup Pineapple Chunks:** Fresh or canned.
- **2 Bell Peppers:** (Red and Green) for visual contrast and bitterness.
- **1 White Onion:** Cut into 1-inch petals.
- **3 cloves Garlic:** Minced.
- **1 tsp Fresh Ginger:** Grated.

## **Equipment**

- **Wok or Large Skillet:** Carbon steel is ideal for "wok hei" (breath of the wok).
- **Spider Strainer:** For safe removal of fried chicken pieces.
- **Digital Thermometer:** To ensure oil stays at 350°F (175°C).

## **How to Make It (Step-by-Step)**

![](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Sweet-&-Sour-Chicken-2-1536.webp)

1. **Velvet the Chicken:** Cut chicken into 1-inch cubes. Toss with soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, and baking soda. Let sit for 20 minutes. This process, known as ++[velveting](https://www.seriouseats.com/chinese-velveting-101-introduction-water-velveting)++, ensures the meat remains tender even after frying.
2. **Coat for Crunch:** Dredge the marinated chicken in cornstarch until every piece is fully isolated and dry to the touch. Shake off excess.
3. **The First Fry:** Heat 2 inches of neutral oil (canola or peanut) to 350°F. Fry chicken in batches for 3–4 minutes until pale golden. Remove and drain.
4. **The Second Fry (Pro Move):** Increase oil heat to 375°F. Flash-fry the chicken for 60 seconds. This drives out residual moisture for a "glass-like" exterior.
5. **Prepare the Sauce:** Whisk the sauce ingredients in a small bowl. Ensure the sugar is mostly dissolved.
6. **Stir-Fry Aromatics:** In a clean wok with 1 tbsp oil, sear the onions and peppers over high heat until charred but crisp (2 minutes). Add garlic and ginger for the last 30 seconds.
7. **Reduce and Glaze:** Pour the sauce into the wok. Bring to a vigorous boil until it thickens into a syrupy consistency.
8. **The Final Toss:** Add the fried chicken and pineapple chunks. Toss rapidly for 30 seconds to coat every piece. Serve immediately to prevent the crust from softening.

## **Pro Tips FOr Sweet & Sour Chicken**

- **Temperature Control:** If the oil drops below 325°F, the chicken will absorb grease and become heavy.
- **Cornstarch vs. Flour:** Always use cornstarch for the coating. Flour contains gluten, which can lead to a chewy, bread-like texture rather than a crisp shell.
- **The "Sizzle" Test:** If the sauce doesn't sizzle and bubble instantly when hitting the wok, your pan isn't hot enough to emulsify the sugars.

## **Common Mistakes to Avoid**

- **Overcrowding the Pan:** Frying too many pieces at once drops the oil temperature, leading to soggy chicken.
- **Burying the Lede with Sauce:** Do not let the chicken sit in the sauce. The goal is a "dry" glaze, not a soup.
- **Using Only Breast Meat:** Without careful timing, breast meat dries out. Thighs are more forgiving for home cooks.

## **Easy Variations**

- **Air Fryer Version:** Spray coated chicken with oil and air fry at 400°F for 12–15 minutes, shaking halfway through.
- **Healthy Swap:** Use honey or maple syrup instead of white sugar, and apple cider vinegar for a different fruity acidity.
- **Protein Pivot:** Substitute chicken with firm tofu (pressed) or jumbo shrimp.

## **Serving Suggestions**

- **Base:** Jasmine rice or egg fried rice.
- **Garnish:** Toasted sesame seeds and thinly sliced scallions (green parts only).
- **Side:** Steamed bok choy or a simple smashed cucumber salad to cut through the sweetness.

## **Storage and Reheating**

- **Storage:** Store in an airtight container for up to 3 days.
- **Reheating:** Avoid the microwave, which turns the coating "rubbery." Reheat in an oven or air fryer at 375°F for 5 minutes to restore crispiness.

## **Frequently Asked Questions**

**Q: Can I make the sauce ahead of time?** A: Yes. The sauce can be whisked and stored in the fridge for up to a week. Shake well before adding to the wok.

**Q: Why is my sauce too thin?** A: You likely didn't let it boil long enough. The sugar and pineapple juice need to reduce to reach a glaze consistency. Alternatively, add a small cornstarch slurry (1 tsp cornstarch + 1 tsp water) to the boiling sauce.

**Q: Is ketchup really authentic?** A: In modern Cantonese home cooking and "Cha Chaan Teng" (Hong Kong cafes), ketchup is frequently used as a convenient source of tomato umami and acidity.

## **Final Note**

Mastering Sweet and Sour Chicken is about managing moisture. By using the **velveting** technique and a **double-fry** method, you elevate a simple takeout dish into a high-end culinary experience. The secret is speed: once the chicken hits the sauce, the clock is ticking—serve it hot, serve it fast.
