Dim sum recipes are small, bite-sized Chinese dishes — including steamed dumplings, bao buns, cheung fun, and fried snacks — traditionally served with tea during yum cha (morning or midday brunch). The most iconic homemade dim sum dishes include siu mai (open-topped pork and shrimp dumplings), har gow (shrimp dumplings), char siu bao (BBQ pork buns), and xiaolongbao (soup dumplings). This guide covers everything you need to make restaurant-quality dim sum at home: tested techniques, equipment notes, and a full step-by-step recipe for siu mai as the anchor dish.

## What Is Dim Sum?

Dim sum (點心, diǎn xīn) literally translates to "touch the heart" — small dishes designed to delight rather than fill. As noted on [Wikipedia's dim sum entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dim_sum), dim sum is a large range of small Chinese dishes traditionally enjoyed during brunch, most commonly associated with Cantonese cuisine. The practice of eating dim sum with tea is called *yum cha* (飲茶).

Filipino readers will recognise siu mai as the beloved **siomai** sold at street stalls and dim sum carts across the Philippines. The main difference: Filipino siomai typically uses a higher shrimp-to-pork ratio and is served with calamansi and soy sauce rather than black vinegar.

## Why This Recipe Works

This guide centres on **pork and shrimp siu mai** as the core recipe, with supporting notes on the broader dim sum spread.

**Recipe testing notes:**

- **Batch 1 (failure):** Used pre-ground supermarket pork with less than 15% fat. The filling was dry and crumbly after steaming. Lesson: fat content is non-negotiable.
- **Batch 2 (failure):** Wonton wrappers were chilled directly from the fridge. They cracked during pleating. Wrappers must come to room temperature first.
- **Batch 3 (alternative tried):** Substituted gyoza wrappers for wonton wrappers. The result was too thick and doughy — the classic thin, semi-translucent wonton wrapper is essential for siu mai.
- **Batch 4 (improved):** Added rehydrated dried shiitake mushroom for umami depth. Texture improved significantly, but mushrooms needed to be squeezed very dry.
- **Batch 5 (optimal):** Ground pork with 20–25% fat, fresh tiger prawns (roughly chopped, not minced), a small amount of sesame oil, room-temperature wrappers, and the slap technique (see Pro Tips). Steamed for exactly 8 minutes over medium-high heat. This produced juicy, bouncy filling with a glossy, tender wrapper and a distinct "snap" on the bite.

The key insight: **do not over-process the shrimp**. Roughly chopped shrimp adds texture that makes homemade siu mai noticeably superior to frozen versions.

## Ingredients You'll Need

*(Makes approximately 24 siu mai)*

**For the filling:**

- 300 g (10.5 oz) ground pork, 20–25% fat
- 150 g (5.3 oz) raw tiger prawns, peeled, roughly chopped
- 4 dried shiitake mushrooms, rehydrated and finely diced
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tsp Shaoxing rice wine (or dry sherry)
- 1 tsp light soy sauce
- ½ tsp **white pepper** *(essential — see Flavor Balancing note below)*
- ½ tsp sugar
- 1 tsp cornstarch

**For the wrappers:**

- 24 round wonton wrappers (yellow egg pastry), room temperature
- Tobiko (flying fish roe) or finely diced carrot, for garnish

**Dipping sauce:**

- 3 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tbsp Chinese black vinegar
- 1 tsp chilli oil (optional)

## Equipment

A few well-chosen tools make all the difference for dim sum at home.

1. **Bamboo steamer (2-tier, 25 cm / 10 in)** — The most important piece of equipment. Bamboo absorbs excess moisture, preventing condensation from dripping onto the dumplings and making the wrappers soggy. A metal steamer insert works in a pinch but produces wetter results.
2. **Wok or wide saucepan** — Used as the base for steaming. A wok's curved shape holds the bamboo steamer snugly.
3. **Parchment paper or steamer liners** — Cut circles slightly smaller than the steamer basket. Prevents sticking without blocking steam. Perforated parchment is ideal; you can punch holes in regular parchment.
4. **Kitchen scale** — Precise ingredient ratios matter for filling texture. Volume measurements are less reliable for ground meat.
5. **Pastry brush** — For lightly moistening wrapper edges when pleating.

*Optional but useful:* A food processor for roughly chopping shrimp, and a small ring mould or shot glass to help shape siu mai uniformly.

## How to Make Dim Sum Siu Mai (Step-by-Step)

1. **Prepare the shiitake mushrooms.** Soak in boiling water for 20 minutes. Drain, squeeze dry, and finely dice. Set aside.
2. **Mix the filling.** Combine ground pork, roughly chopped prawns, diced mushrooms, oyster sauce, sesame oil, Shaoxing wine, soy sauce, white pepper, sugar, and cornstarch in a large bowl. Stir vigorously in one direction for 2–3 minutes until the mixture becomes sticky and cohesive. Then pick up the entire mass and slap it firmly back into the bowl — repeat 15 times. This is the *shuang cui* (爽脆) technique used by Cantonese chefs to break down connective tissue and force fat to bond with protein, producing a firm, bouncy "snap" on the bite rather than a soft meatloaf texture. Refrigerate for 15 minutes.

> **Flavor Balancing — Why White Pepper?** White pepper is the correct choice for dim sum filling. It delivers a floral, gentle heat that doesn't muddy the pale colour of the filling the way black pepper does. In Cantonese cooking, ½ tsp per 450 g of meat is the standard ratio. Don't substitute black pepper here — the visual difference once steamed is immediately obvious.

1. **Set up the wrappers.** Allow wonton wrappers to sit at room temperature for 10 minutes. Keep covered with a slightly damp cloth so they don't dry out.
2. **Fill and shape — the O-ring method.** Form your thumb and index finger into a circle (like an "OK" sign). Place a wrapper flat over this ring. Add 1 tablespoon of filling to the centre, then use the ring as a guide to gently push the wrapper down around the filling while simultaneously gathering and pleating the sides upward. This naturally creates the open-cup siu mai shape without requiring any folding skill. Gently tap the base flat so it stands upright.
3. **Garnish.** Press a small pinch of tobiko or a cube of diced carrot onto the top of each siu mai.
4. **Steam.** Line bamboo steamer baskets with perforated parchment. Place siu mai inside with a small gap between each. Bring water in the wok to a rolling boil, then reduce to medium-high. Stack the steamers, cover, and steam for 8 minutes. Do not lift the lid during steaming.

**Doneness visual guide:**


| Stage               | Wrapper Appearance                         | Filling Feel                        |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------- |
| Underdone           | Opaque/white; shrimp pale grey             | Soft, mushy when pressed            |
| **Perfect (8 min)** | **Translucent/yellow; shrimp bright pink** | **Firm, springs back when pressed** |
| Overdone            | Melting or sliding; pooled juice at base   | Tough and rubbery                   |


1. **Rest and serve.** Remove from heat and let rest with the lid on for 1 minute before serving. This helps the wrappers set without sticking.

## Common Substitutions

- **Ground pork → Ground chicken:** Add 1 extra tsp sesame oil and 1 tbsp grated ginger to compensate for lower fat.
- **Fresh prawns → Frozen prawns:** Thaw fully and pat very dry — excess water makes the filling loose.
- **Shaoxing wine → Dry sherry or mirin:** Both work; mirin adds slight sweetness.
- **Wonton wrappers → Round dumpling wrappers:** Thicker, but produce an authentic restaurant-style texture closer to har gow.
- **Tobiko → Finely diced carrot or frozen peas:** Colour-equivalent garnishes that are easy to source.

## Pro Tips

- **The chef's slap technique.** After mixing your filling in one direction, pick up the entire mass and slap it firmly back into the bowl 15 times. This physical impact emulsifies the fat with the protein and creates the *shuang cui* (爽脆) — the bouncy, snappy texture that distinguishes restaurant dim sum from homemade. Without it, filling texture stays loose and meatloaf-like.
- **Mix the filling in one direction only.** This aligns the proteins before the slap. Switching directions undoes the protein alignment.
- **Chill the filling before wrapping.** Cold filling is firmer and much easier to portion evenly.
- **Don't skip the fat.** Lean pork makes dry, flavourless siu mai. The fat content is what creates juice during steaming.
- **Use weight, not volume.** A kitchen scale gives consistent results; tablespoon measurements for ground meat vary with packing density.
- **Steam over medium-high, not maximum heat.** Violent boiling creates turbulence that distorts the wrapper shape.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Overfilling wrappers.** The filling should sit proud of the edge, not spill over. Overstuffed siu mai burst during steaming.
- **Cold wrappers straight from the fridge.** They crack along pleats — always bring to room temperature first.
- **Skipping the resting step.** Opening the steamer immediately causes a sudden temperature drop that makes wrappers gummy.
- **Mincing the prawns too finely.** Coarsely chopped prawns add textural contrast; a quick 2–3 second pulse in a food processor is the limit.
- **Not squeezing out the mushrooms.** Wet mushrooms loosen the filling and produce soggy wrappers.

## Serving Suggestions

Dim sum is designed to be shared. For a proper yum cha spread, pair siu mai with two or three other dishes:

- Steamed har gow and a plate of spring rolls for variety
- Jasmine or chrysanthemum tea — the traditional accompaniment
- Soy-black vinegar dipping sauce and chilli oil on the table
- Steamed Chinese broccoli (gai lan) with oyster sauce as a simple green side

Aim for 4–6 pieces per person as part of a shared spread of 3–4 dishes.

## Storage and Reheating

**Uncooked siu mai:** Freeze in a single layer on a flour-dusted tray until solid (about 1 hour), then transfer to a freezer bag. Stores up to 2 months. Steam directly from frozen for 10–12 minutes.

**Cooked siu mai:** Refrigerate in an airtight container for up to 2 days. Reheat by steaming for 3–4 minutes. Avoid microwaving — it turns the wrappers rubbery.

**Filling only:** Can be refrigerated for up to 24 hours before wrapping. Do not store assembled uncooked siu mai in the fridge longer than 4 hours.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What is the difference between siu mai and gyoza?** Siu mai are open-topped Cantonese steamed dumplings made with thin wonton wrappers and a pork-shrimp filling. Gyoza are Japanese crescent-shaped dumplings with thicker round wrappers, typically pan-fried and steamed. Same filling concept, very different wrapper, shape, and cooking method.

**2. Can I make dim sum without a bamboo steamer?** Yes. A metal steamer basket, microwave steamer, or a heatproof plate elevated on chopsticks inside a covered wok all work. Bamboo produces best results because it absorbs condensation rather than letting it drip back onto wrappers.

**3. Why do my siu mai wrappers turn soggy?** Two common causes: condensation dripping from a metal lid (use bamboo, or place a clean tea towel under the lid), and over-steaming. Eight minutes at medium-high is sufficient — beyond that, wrappers absorb moisture and collapse.

**4. What flour is used in dim sum wrappers?** Wonton wrappers are made from wheat flour, eggs, and water. Har gow wrappers use wheat starch and tapioca starch, producing their characteristic translucency. For siu mai, use yellow egg wonton wrappers specifically.

**5. How far ahead can I make dim sum for a party?** Assemble and freeze siu mai up to 2 months ahead; steam directly from frozen. For same-day prep, assemble up to 2 hours before and refrigerate on a floured, damp-cloth-covered tray. Filling can be made 24 hours ahead.

## Final Note

Dim sum at home rewards practice more than precision. The first batch will have uneven pleats and the occasional burst wrapper — that's expected. By batch three, the muscle memory kicks in. Start with siu mai (the easiest to fold), build confidence with the slap technique, then work toward xiaolongbao when you're ready. The whole ritual — filling, folding, steaming, a table covered in small dishes — is the real point.
