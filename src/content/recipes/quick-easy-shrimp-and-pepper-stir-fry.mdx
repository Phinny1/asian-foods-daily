## What Is Shrimp and pepper stir fry?

Shrimp and pepper stir fry is a classic Cantonese-style dish where large prawns are cooked over intense heat with sliced bell peppers, aromatics, and a savory sauce built on soy, oyster sauce, and rice vinegar. It belongs to the family of Chinese stir-fried seafood dishes that rely on fast, high-temperature cooking — known in Chinese cuisine as *chao* (炒). According to [Wikipedia's overview of stir frying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stir_frying), the technique dates back to the Han dynasty and remains one of the most efficient cooking methods for preserving texture and nutrition. The key differentiator in this version is velveting — a Cantonese prep technique that gives the shrimp their silky, restaurant-quality finish.

## Why This Recipe Works

This recipe was tested across four distinct batches before reaching its final form.

**Batch 1 — Baseline:** Medium (41/50) shrimp, mixed pepper strips, basic soy and sesame oil. Shrimp overcooked before peppers softened. Texture: rubbery throughout.

**Batch 2 — Velveting and shrimp size:** Switched to large (21/25) shrimp. Introduced a quick velvet — a pinch of cornstarch and neutral oil tossed on the raw shrimp before cooking. This coating protects proteins from direct heat shock, producing the silky texture found in Cantonese restaurant stir fries. Pre-charring peppers separately for 90 seconds before adding shrimp resolved the timing mismatch entirely.

**Batch 3 — Sauce depth:** Added oyster sauce, a cornstarch slurry, and rice vinegar. The vinegar brightened the overall flavor significantly. Sauce clung properly; velveted shrimp held coating through the toss far better than un-velveted batches.

**Batch 4 — Heat and timing (optimal):** Wok preheated until smoking. 90-second per side sear on velveted shrimp. Sauce added briefly off heat to prevent oyster sauce sugars from scorching. Result: tender, glossy shrimp with lightly charred diamond-cut pepper edges and a well-balanced glaze.

**What failed:** Adding pre-mixed sauce before shrimp finished cooking created a gummy, steamed finish. Marinating shrimp longer than 15 minutes in soy sauce broke down proteins regardless of velveting, producing mushy texture.

## Shrimp and pepper stir fry Ingredients

**For the shrimp:**

- 500g (1 lb) large raw shrimp, 21/25 count, peeled and deveined
- 1 tsp cornstarch (for velveting)
- 1 tsp neutral oil (for velveting)
- Salt and white pepper to taste

**For the stir fry:**

- 1 red bell pepper, diamond cut
- 1 green bell pepper, diamond cut
- 1 yellow bell pepper, diamond cut
- 4 garlic cloves, minced
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 2 tbsp neutral cooking oil (avocado or vegetable)
- 2 spring onions, sliced (garnish)

**For the sauce:**

- 2 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tbsp rice vinegar
- 1 tsp cornstarch + 2 tbsp cold water (slurry, mixed separately)

## Equipment

🥘 **Carbon steel wok or large skillet** — A 14-inch flat-bottomed carbon steel wok is the ideal tool. It heats fast, holds extreme temperatures, and delivers the caramelization needed for wok hei. Non-stick pans are unsafe at stir-fry temperatures and produced inferior char in testing. Cast iron works but cools too quickly when shrimp are added in batches.

🔪 **Sharp chef's knife or Chinese cleaver** — Needed for precise diamond cuts. A 7–8 inch chef's knife handles the diagonal cuts cleanly; a cleaver works faster once you are comfortable with it.

🍳 **Metal wok spatula (chuan)** — A flat-edged metal spatula allows fast scraping and tossing without breaking shrimp apart. Silicone spatulas flex too much under the forceful movements high-heat stir frying requires.

🥣 **Small prep bowls** — Mise en place is not optional here. Sauce, slurry, and aromatics must all be pre-measured and within arm's reach before the wok goes on. Stir frying moves faster than any measuring can keep up with.

🔥 **Wok lid or large baking sheet** — A safety essential. Keep it within arm's reach whenever cooking at high heat. If oil flares, placing the lid over the wok smothers it immediately. **Never add water to a hot oil flare.**

## How to Make It Shrimp and pepper stir fry (Step-by-Step)

> ⚠️ **Safety first:** Before you begin, position a wok lid or flat baking sheet beside the stove. A smoking-hot wok with oil can flare or spatter. A lid placed over the top instantly controls it. Never pour water on hot oil.

1. **Velvet the shrimp.** **Pat** shrimp completely dry with paper towels — surface moisture is the enemy of a good sear. **Season** with a pinch of salt and white pepper. **Toss** in 1 tsp cornstarch and 1 tsp neutral oil until each shrimp is lightly coated. **Rest** for 5 minutes maximum before cooking.
  ![ Raw large shrimp coated in cornstarch and oil for velveting on a matte charcoal ceramic plate, step one of shrimp and pepper stir fry preparation](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-a-1536.webp)
2. **Mix the sauce.** **Stir** together soy sauce, oyster sauce, rice vinegar, and sesame oil in a small bowl until combined. **Prepare** the cornstarch slurry in a separate bowl — keep them apart until Step 9.
  ![Stir fry sauce of soy sauce, oyster sauce, rice vinegar and sesame oil in a matte slate ceramic bowl beside a cornstarch slurry, step two of shrimp and pepper stir fry](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-b-1536.webp)
3. **Diamond-cut the peppers.** **Slice** each pepper on the diagonal into triangular pieces roughly 3–4 cm. This cut creates larger flat surfaces that make full contact with the wok bottom, producing better char marks and keeping pieces structurally firm through the sauce toss. Standard strips lose surface contact and overcook unevenly.
  ![Red, yellow and green bell peppers diamond-cut into triangular pieces on a dark slate surface, step three of shrimp and pepper stir fry preparation](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-c-1536.webp)
4. **Heat the wok.** **Set** over the highest burner available. **Heat** dry for 2–3 minutes until wisps of smoke appear. **Add** 2 tbsp oil and **swirl** to coat the entire cooking surface.
  ![Seasoned carbon steel wok interior coated with a thin film of neutral oil, preheated and ready for high-heat stir frying, step four of shrimp and pepper stir fry](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-d-1536.webp)
5. **Char the peppers.** **Add** all diamond-cut peppers at once. **Leave** them undisturbed for 30–40 seconds so flat sides contact the wok and char. **Toss** and stir fry for a total of 90 seconds — edges should be lightly blackened, flesh still firm. **Transfer** to a plate.
  ![Diamond-cut red and yellow bell peppers with char marks and caramelized edges stir frying in a carbon steel wok, step five of shrimp and pepper stir fry](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-e-1536.webp)
6. **Sear the shrimp.** **Add** a splash more oil if needed. **Arrange** velveted shrimp in a single layer — do not crowd. **Sear** undisturbed for 60–90 seconds until edges turn pink and golden. **Flip** and sear the second side for 60 seconds. **Work in two batches** if your wok is smaller than 14 inches.
  ![Velveted large shrimp with golden caramelized crust searing in a single layer in a carbon steel wok, step six of shrimp and pepper stir fry](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-f-1536.webp)
7. **Add aromatics.** **Push** shrimp to the outer edge. **Drop** garlic and ginger into the wok centre. **Stir** continuously for 15–20 seconds until fragrant. At this temperature, garlic burns in under 30 seconds — do not leave it unattended.
  ![Freshly minced garlic and grated ginger added to the centre of a carbon steel wok during shrimp and pepper stir fry cooking, step seven](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-g-1536.webp)
8. **Combine everything.** **Return** charred peppers to the wok. **Pour** the sauce over everything. **Toss** all ingredients together vigorously for 30 seconds.
  ![Seared shrimp, charred bell peppers and oyster sauce glaze combined in a carbon steel wok, step eight of quick and easy shrimp and pepper stir fry](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-h-1536.webp)
9. **Thicken the sauce.** **Pour** in the cornstarch slurry. **Toss** for 20–30 seconds until the sauce turns glossy and clings to every shrimp and pepper piece evenly.
10. **Plate and serve.** **Remove** from heat immediately — shrimp continue cooking from residual heat. **Spoon** over steamed jasmine rice and **scatter** sliced spring onions on top.
  ![Finished quick and easy shrimp and pepper stir fry served over steamed jasmine rice in a matte charcoal ceramic bowl, garnished with sliced spring onions](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Easy-Shrimp-and-Pepper-Stir-Fry-j-1536.webp)

## Common Substitutions

- **No oyster sauce?** Use hoisin sauce, or combine 1 tbsp soy sauce with ½ tsp brown sugar and a drop of Worcestershire sauce for a close approximation.
- **Frozen shrimp?** Thaw overnight in the fridge or under cold running water. Pat thoroughly dry before velveting — any remaining moisture causes steaming instead of searing.
- **No fresh ginger?** Use ½ tsp ground ginger. The flavor is slightly less bright but still works in the sauce.
- **Reducing sodium?** Swap regular soy sauce for low-sodium tamari and reduce to 1.5 tbsp. The oyster sauce already contributes saltiness.
- **Vegetarian version?** Replace shrimp with extra-firm tofu cut into 2 cm cubes, or king oyster mushrooms torn lengthwise. Apply the cornstarch velvet the same way — it creates a crisp outer layer on tofu that holds the sauce well.

## Pro Tips

- **Dry shrimp = better sear, every time.** Surface moisture creates steam the moment it hits hot oil, preventing the Maillard reaction that produces golden, caramelized edges. Patting dry before velveting removes this barrier entirely.
- **Velvet 5 minutes before, not 30.** The cornstarch coating draws moisture from the shrimp over time and loses adhesion. Apply it right before the wok goes on.
- **Diamond cut is not just aesthetic.** The angled slices give bell peppers significantly more flat surface area in contact with the wok. More contact = more char = better flavor and a more structurally sound piece that stays crisp when tossed in sauce.
- **Pull the wok back before adding sauce.** Oyster sauce contains natural sugars that scorch within seconds at full wok temperature. A brief pull off the flame before pouring prevents bitter, burnt-tasting sauce.
- **Use the C-shape test for shrimp doneness.** A loose C means slightly undercooked — 15 more seconds. A firm C means perfect. A tight O means overcooked — pull them immediately.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Skipping the velvet** — The cornstarch-oil coating is what separates silky restaurant shrimp from rubbery home versions. It takes 60 seconds and makes a measurable difference.
- **Overcrowding the pan** — Too many shrimp at once drops wok temperature sharply, turning a sear into a steam. Two batches always beats one crowded batch.
- **Low or medium heat** — Stir frying is a high-heat technique by definition. Medium heat produces pale, steamed ingredients with no caramelization and no wok hei character.
- **Early garlic** — Minced garlic burns in under 30 seconds in a smoking wok. Add it only after the shrimp are partially cooked and pushed to the side.
- **No cornstarch slurry** — Without it the sauce is thin and slides off every piece, pooling at the base of the pan. The slurry is what creates the glossy, restaurant-style coating.

## Easy Variations

**Spicy shrimp and pepper stir fry** — Add 1–2 tsp chili garlic sauce or 1 tsp dried chili flakes with the aromatics. The velvet coating holds the heat-infused sauce especially well, making each shrimp evenly spicy.

**Citrus shrimp and pepper stir fry** — Add the zest of one orange and swap rice vinegar for 2 tbsp fresh orange juice. This works beautifully with yellow and red peppers and gives the glaze a bright, sweet-savory character.

**Garlic butter shrimp stir fry** — Stir a small knob of cold unsalted butter into the sauce just before plating and squeeze over fresh lemon. Creates a glossy, Western-fusion finish without altering the core technique.

**Shrimp, pepper, and mushroom stir fry** — Add 150g sliced shiitake mushrooms alongside the bell peppers in Step 5. They absorb the oyster sauce glaze deeply and add an earthy, umami layer — similar to the mushroom-and-pepper approach used in the [epic Chinese beef stir fry](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-epic-chinese-beef-stir-fry-at-home/).

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 2 days. Spread shrimp flat before refrigerating — residual heat in a pile continues cooking them and toughens the texture.

**Freezer:** Not recommended. Cooked shrimp become rubbery and waterlogged when frozen and thawed; the velvet coating dissolves entirely. Cook fresh each time.

**Reheating:** Best method is a hot skillet with 1–2 tbsp water added to loosen the sauce, tossed over medium-high heat for 2 minutes. Microwave reheating works but dries out the shrimp. Do not reheat more than once.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Can I use pre-cooked shrimp in this stir fry?** Yes — add pre-cooked shrimp in the last 30–45 seconds of cooking, after the sauce has been added, and toss just long enough to coat and warm through. Skip the velveting step. Pre-cooked shrimp toughen quickly, so do not let them sit in the hot wok longer than necessary.

**2. What is velveting and do I need to do it?** Velveting means coating raw shrimp in a thin layer of cornstarch and neutral oil before cooking. This takes under one minute and produces noticeably silkier, more tender shrimp — the same texture you get at Cantonese restaurants. For this recipe, no egg white is needed, just cornstarch and oil. It is the single most effective upgrade you can make to a home stir fry.

**3. Why are my shrimp rubbery even when I velvet them?** Rubbery shrimp after velveting almost always means overcooking. The velvet coating extends the window of perfect doneness but does not eliminate it. Watch the shape: a firm C means done; a tight O means overcooked. Pull them from the wok the moment each shrimp curls into a firm C and the flesh turns fully opaque.

**4. Why use a diamond cut on the peppers instead of strips?** Cutting bell peppers diagonally into triangular pieces rather than straight strips creates more flat surface area per piece. More surface contact with the wok bottom means more caramelization, better char marks, and a firmer texture that holds up through the sauce toss. Strips have narrow ends that overcook before the wider middle section has charred.

**5. What can I use instead of a wok?** The best non-wok option is a 12-inch carbon steel or cast iron skillet, preheated thoroughly before oil goes in. Avoid non-stick cookware — most non-stick coatings degrade and release fumes above 230°C (450°F), which is below the temperature needed for a proper stir fry sear.
