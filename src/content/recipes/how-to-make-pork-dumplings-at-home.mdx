**Pork dumplings (jiaozi) are made by combining ground pork, cabbage, ginger, garlic, soy sauce, and sesame oil in a thin wheat-flour wrapper, then pan-frying, steaming, or boiling until cooked through. Ready in under 90 minutes, they freeze beautifully and taste far better than store-bought.**

## What Is  Pork Dumplings?

Dumplings arrived in China over 1,800 years ago, evolving from simple wheat parcels to the sophisticated jiaozi of the Tang Dynasty — a period when dumpling shape and pleating carried symbolic meaning. The crescent form mimicked ancient Chinese currency, making dumplings traditional fare for Lunar New Year celebrations when prosperity was invoked. Today, those Tang Dynasty shapes would be instantly recognizable on any dim sum cart — a testament to how little this dish needed to change. Food historians confirm that dumplings from the Tang period looked nearly identical to what's served in restaurants today, as explored in [Smithsonian Magazine's investigation into dumpling history](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/how-old-are-dumplings-1743611/). ## Why This Recipe Works

**Recipe Testing Notes**

**Batch 1 — Store-bought wrappers, basic filling:** Used commercial dumpling wrappers and unseasoned ground pork with raw cabbage. Result: wrappers tore during pleating and the filling was bland and watery. Two problems identified — pre-made wrappers lack pliability, and raw unsalted cabbage releases moisture during cooking, saturating the wrapper from the inside.

**Batch 2 — Hot water dough, improved seasoning:** Switched to homemade hot water dough and added ginger, garlic, soy sauce, and sesame oil to the filling. Wrappers were dramatically easier to work with and held pleats cleanly. Filling still too wet — cabbage releasing liquid inside the wrapper caused two dumplings to burst during boiling.

**Batch 3 — Salted, squeezed cabbage:** Introduced the salt-and-squeeze technique: tossed napa cabbage with salt, rested 10 minutes, then squeezed out all liquid by hand. Result: noticeably juicier, more cohesive filling with no bursting. Established as the base recipe going forward.

**Batch 4 — Cold water dough (failure):** Tested cold tap water dough to see if the hot water step was truly necessary. Result: tough, elastic wrappers that tore during rolling and sprang back constantly. The gluten network developed too strongly without heat to partially relax it — rolling thin became nearly impossible. Cold water dough was eliminated entirely.

**Batch 5 — Optimal result:** Returned to hot water dough with a full 30-minute rest, 70/30 pork-to-cabbage ratio, fully squeezed cabbage, and directional filling mixing. Result: pliable wrappers that rolled thin effortlessly, clean pleating, and a juicy, well-seasoned filling that held together perfectly during both pan-frying and boiling.

**Why It Works**

Hot water partially denatures gluten proteins before the dough is even kneaded, creating a tender, pliable structure that rolls thin without springing back. The 30-minute rest allows remaining gluten strands to relax further. The 70/30 pork-to-cabbage ratio balances fat richness with moisture absorption — the cabbage soaks up rendered pork fat during cooking, keeping every bite juicy without making the wrapper soggy. Mixing the filling in one direction builds a loose protein network that gives it a cohesive, bouncy texture rather than a crumbly one.

## Pork Dumplings Ingredients

**For the dough (makes ~30 wrappers):**

- 2 cups all-purpose flour
- ¾ cup just-boiled water
- ¼ tsp salt

**For the filling:**

- 300g (10 oz) ground pork (80/20 fat ratio)
- 1 cup napa cabbage, finely chopped
- 1 tsp salt (for cabbage)
- 2 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 2 garlic cloves, minced
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp sesame oil
- 1 tsp rice wine or dry sherry
- 1 egg white
- White pepper to taste

**Dipping sauce:**

- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp rice vinegar
- ½ tsp chili oil
- ½ tsp sesame oil

## Equipment

- **Rolling pin** — A thin wooden dowel-style pin gives better control than a French pin for rolling small wrappers
- **Large mixing bowls (x2)** — One for dough, one for filling
- **Non-stick skillet (12-inch)** — Tested both bamboo steamer and skillet; non-stick delivers superior crispy bottoms for potstickers
- **Bench scraper** — Essential for dividing dough cleanly into equal portions
- **Parchment-lined tray** — For holding filled dumplings before cooking without sticking
- **Kitchen scale** — For consistent portioning (15g dough per wrapper)

## How to Make  Pork Dumplings (Step-by-Step)

> ⚠️ **Safety Note:** The dough requires just-boiled water (100°C/212°F). Allow it to cool 2–3 minutes before kneading by hand, or use a stand mixer with a dough hook to avoid burns. When pan-frying, add water to hot oil carefully — it will splatter violently. Stand back and cover the pan immediately.

1. **Salt the cabbage.** Toss chopped napa cabbage with 1 tsp salt, rest 10 minutes, then squeeze hard with both hands until no liquid remains when pressed. This step is non-negotiable.
2. **Make the dough.** Combine flour and salt. Add just-boiled water gradually, mixing with chopsticks until shaggy. Knead 5–7 minutes until smooth. Cover with a damp cloth and rest 30 minutes.
3. **Mix the filling.** Combine pork, squeezed cabbage, ginger, garlic, soy sauce, sesame oil, rice wine, and egg white. Mix in one direction only for 2 minutes until cohesive and slightly sticky.
4. **Roll the wrappers.** Divide dough into 30 equal pieces (~15g each). Roll each into a circle roughly 3 inches in diameter, slightly thicker at the center than the edges.
5. **Fill and pleat.** Place 1 heaping teaspoon of filling in the center. Fold wrapper in half, pinch the center, then create 3–4 pleats on each side pressing firmly to seal. Use a barely damp fingertip to seal — avoid floury fingers.
6. **Cook your dumplings — choose your method:** **Pan-fry (potsticker method):** Heat oil to 350°F/175°C (medium-high) until shimmering — if it smokes, it's too hot; remove from heat 30 seconds before adding dumplings. Add dumplings flat-side down, cook 2 minutes until golden. Add ¼ cup water all at once, cover immediately, steam 6–7 minutes. Remove lid, cook 1 more minute to re-crisp bottoms. **Boil:** Bring a large pot of water to a gentle boil. Add dumplings in batches, stirring gently to prevent sticking. Cook 6–8 minutes until dumplings float and the dough turns translucent. Remove with a slotted spoon. **Steam:** Line a bamboo steamer basket with parchment paper. Arrange dumplings without touching each other. Steam over simmering water for 10–12 minutes until dough is translucent and filling is cooked through.

## Common Substitutions

- ⭐ **Ground chicken or turkey** instead of pork — same technique, lighter result; add 1 tsp extra sesame oil for moisture
- **Savoy cabbage** if napa is unavailable — same salting method applies
- **Tamari** instead of soy sauce for a gluten-free filling
- **Store-bought gyoza wrappers** if short on time; reduce steaming by 1–2 minutes as they are thinner
- **Chives** can replace half the cabbage for a more pungent, Northern Chinese–style flavor

## Pro Tips

- Mix filling in **one direction only** — builds a cohesive, bouncy texture rather than a crumbly one
- Rest dough for **at least 30 minutes** — it becomes dramatically easier to roll thin without tearing
- **Freeze uncooked dumplings** on a parchment tray first before bagging — prevents clumping
- A **barely damp fingertip** seals wrappers better than a flour-dusted one
- Make the dipping sauce **before** you start assembling — dumplings should be eaten the moment they're done

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Skipping the cabbage squeeze** — wet filling causes wrappers to burst during cooking
- **Overfilling** — 1 teaspoon maximum; more makes proper sealing impossible
- **Using cold water for dough** — produces tough, elastic wrappers that tear during rolling (Batch 4 confirmed this)
- **Cooking frozen dumplings without adjusting time** — add 2–3 extra minutes of steaming
- **Moving dumplings too soon in the pan** — let them release naturally; forcing them tears the skin

## Troubleshooting

**Wrappers tear during rolling:** Dough hasn't rested long enough. Cover and rest 10 more minutes. If still tearing, the dough is too dry — add ½ tsp water, knead 1 minute, rest again before rolling.

**Filling bursts during cooking:** Cabbage wasn't squeezed sufficiently, or dumplings were overfilled. Next batch, squeeze until absolutely no liquid comes out when pressed hard. Reduce filling to ¾ tsp per wrapper.

**Dumplings stick to the pan:** Pan wasn't hot enough before adding oil, or dumplings were moved too early. Oil should shimmer before dumplings go in. Don't attempt to move them until the golden crust releases the dumpling naturally — usually after 2 full minutes.

## Easy Variations

## Storage and Reheating

- **Refrigerator:** Cooked dumplings keep up to 3 days in an airtight container. Re-crisp in a dry non-stick pan over medium heat for 3–4 minutes.
- **Freezer:** Freeze uncooked dumplings on a parchment-lined tray for 1 hour first, then transfer to a zip-lock bag. Cook straight from frozen — no thawing needed. Add 2–3 minutes to steaming time.
- **Avoid microwaving** — wrappers turn gummy and bottoms go soft.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Can I make pork dumplings without a rolling pin?** Yes — a smooth wine bottle or drinking glass works well. Roll from the center outward, rotating the wrapper a quarter turn after each pass to keep it even and slightly thicker in the middle.

**2. Why do my dumpling wrappers keep tearing?** Almost always a resting issue. The dough needs at least 30 minutes covered to let gluten relax. If they still tear after resting, the dough is too dry — add ½ tsp water at a time, knead briefly, and rest again before rolling.

**3. Can I make the filling the night before?** Yes. The filling refrigerates well for up to 24 hours. The flavors deepen overnight and the filling firms up slightly, making it easier to portion and pleat cleanly.

**4. What's the difference between jiaozi and gyoza?** Chinese jiaozi use a thicker, chewier dough and are boiled, steamed, or pan-fried with a steam step. Japanese gyoza use a thinner wrapper, are always pan-fried, and tend to feature more garlic and a finer filling texture. Our [How to Make Gyoza](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-gyoza/) recipe is a great side-by-side comparison.

**5. How do I know when pan-fried dumplings are fully cooked?** After the 6–7 minute covered steam, the dough will look translucent and the filling will have puffed slightly inside the wrapper. For certainty, the internal temperature of the filling should reach 74°C (165°F). Cut one open — if the pork is opaque throughout with no pink, they're done.
