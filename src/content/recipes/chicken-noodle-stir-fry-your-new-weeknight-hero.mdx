**Chicken Noodle Stir Fry** is a fast, one-pan Asian dinner made with tender sliced chicken, egg noodles, vegetables, and a savory soy-oyster sauce. It comes together in **under 30 minutes**, uses pantry-staple ingredients, and is endlessly customizable. Cook the chicken first over high heat, set aside, stir-fry vegetables, add pre-cooked noodles and sauce, toss everything together, and serve immediately.

## What Is **Chicken Noodle Stir Fry**?

Chicken Noodle Stir Fry is a high-heat, wok-based dish rooted in Chinese cooking traditions. Thinly sliced chicken is velveted for tenderness, then cooked with crisp vegetables and noodles in a glossy umami sauce. Unlike soupy noodle bowls, every strand is coated in sauce rather than submerged in broth — think [Chinese Egg Stir Fry Noodles](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/chinese-egg-stir-fry-noodles/) taken to a heartier, protein-forward level. It is equally at home on a Tuesday night as it is at a casual dinner table.

## **Chicken Noodle Stir Fry Ingredients**

**Serves 4 | Prep: 20 min | Cook: 10 min | Total: 30 min**

**Protein**

- 400g (14 oz) chicken breast or thighs, thinly sliced

**Velveting Marinade**

- 1 tbsp cornstarch
- 1 egg white
- 1 tsp soy sauce
- 1 tsp sesame oil

**Noodles**

- 300g (10 oz) fresh egg noodles (or 200g dried)

**Vegetables**

- 1 cup bean sprouts
- 1 medium carrot, julienned
- 2 scallions, cut into 2-inch pieces
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated

**Sauce**

- 3 tbsp soy sauce
- 2 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce (for color)
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tsp sugar
- 2 tbsp neutral oil for cooking

## Common Substitutions

- **Chicken** → shrimp, tofu, or thinly sliced beef
- **Egg noodles** → rice noodles or ramen are the closest swap in texture and sauce absorption; udon works well for a chewier result (see [Chicken Udon Noodles](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/chicken-udon-noodles-recipe-easy-comfort-food-for-weeknights/) for udon-specific technique). Spaghetti is a last-resort option only — it clumps badly, absorbs sauce unevenly, and won't char in the wok. If you use it, cook 2 minutes under al dente and toss immediately in oil after draining.
- **Oyster sauce** → hoisin sauce or extra soy sauce
- **Bean sprouts** → shredded cabbage or snap peas
- **Fresh ginger** → ¼ tsp ground ginger

> **No wok? No problem.** Cast iron is the best substitute — preheat it empty for 5 full minutes before adding oil. Stainless steel works but needs more oil and patience. Non-stick is usable but will never achieve the caramelized edges that make this dish sing. **On induction:** use a flat-bottomed carbon steel wok only — round-bottomed woks won't make full contact with the surface, heat unevenly, and won't get hot enough to sear properly.

> **Testing note:** A carbon steel wok, properly seasoned and preheated 2 full minutes, produced dramatically better results than non-stick. ## How to Make **Chicken Noodle Stir Fry** (Step-by-Step)

1. **Velvet the chicken.** Combine sliced chicken with cornstarch, egg white, soy sauce, and sesame oil. Mix well and rest 10 minutes at room temperature — cold protein seizes on contact with a hot wok and releases moisture, turning your stir fry watery. Room temp chicken sears cleanly.
2. **Cook the noodles.** Boil to 1 minute under package instructions. Drain, rinse with cold water, and toss with a drizzle of oil to prevent clumping.
3. **Mix the sauce.** Whisk together soy sauce, oyster sauce, dark soy sauce, sesame oil, and sugar in a small bowl. Set aside.
4. **Sear the chicken.** Heat wok over high heat until smoking. Add 1 tbsp oil and cook chicken in a single layer, undisturbed, 90 seconds. Flip and cook another 60 seconds. *Done when: edges are caramelized and the center is just barely pink — carryover cooking finishes it off the heat.* Remove and set aside.
5. **Stir-fry aromatics and vegetables.** Add remaining oil to the wok. Add garlic and ginger — 30 seconds. Add carrot — 1 minute. Add bean sprouts and scallions — 30 seconds.
6. **Add noodles.** Toss noodles into the wok. Spread and press against the hot surface for 60 seconds to get some color. *Ready when: slightly charred in spots and making a light tapping sound when tossed — that's the crust forming.*
7. **Finish with sauce.** Once noodles are hot and starting to char at the edges, return chicken to the wok. Pour sauce over everything. Toss vigorously for 90 seconds until every strand is glossy and the sauce has reduced slightly. Add sauce here — not earlier — to avoid burning.
8. **Serve immediately.** Garnish with extra scallion, sesame seeds, or a drizzle of chili oil.

## Pro Tips

- **Don't crowd the wok.** Packed ingredients drop the pan temperature and release steam instead of searing — you get braised, soggy results. Cook chicken in a single layer; if doubling the recipe, work in batches.
- **Prep everything before you heat the pan.** Stir fry moves fast — mise en place is non-negotiable.
- **Use the highest heat your stove allows.** This is the single biggest difference between restaurant and home results, as noted by culinary experts at [Serious Eats](https://www.seriouseats.com/best-woks-5218113).
- **A dry wok is a hot wok.** Never add wet ingredients directly from the colander.
- **Dark soy sauce is optional but impactful.** It adds color and slight depth without overpowering saltiness.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Adding sauce too early** — it burns before the noodles are hot
- **Using cold chicken straight from the fridge** — uneven cooking; bring to room temp first
- **Overcooking vegetables** — they should stay crisp, not limp
- **Skipping the velveting step** — this is what separates restaurant texture from home texture
- **Under-drying noodles** — excess water steams instead of fries

## Storage and Reheating

- **Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container up to 3 days
- **Freezer:** Not recommended — noodles become mushy
- **Reheat:** Add 1–2 tbsp water or broth to the pan on medium-high heat; toss until hot (2–3 minutes). Microwave works but loses texture.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Can I use dried noodles instead of fresh?** Yes. Cook dried egg noodles to al dente — about 1 minute less than the package says — then drain, rinse, and oil them before adding to the wok. Dried noodles absorb sauce more aggressively, so you may want to add an extra splash of soy sauce.

**2. What is velveting and do I have to do it?** Velveting is a Chinese technique of marinating protein in cornstarch and egg white before cooking. It creates a protective coating that keeps chicken moist and silky even at high heat. It takes 10 minutes and is strongly recommended — skipping it is the most common reason home stir fry tastes dry.

**3. Can I make this gluten-free?** Yes. Use rice noodles, replace soy sauce with tamari, and use a gluten-free oyster sauce or hoisin. The technique remains the same.

**4. Why does my stir fry turn out watery?** Four culprits: wet noodles (not fully drained), low heat (steaming instead of frying), too much sauce added too early, or an overcrowded pan (see Pro Tips for why this matters). Pat noodles dry, crank the heat, add sauce only at the finish, and cook in batches if needed.

**5. Can I prep this ahead of time?** Partially. Velvet and marinate the chicken up to 24 hours ahead. Mix the sauce and julienne vegetables the morning of. Cook noodles up to 2 hours ahead and toss in oil. Full stir fry should be done fresh — it takes only 15 minutes once prepped.
