**Mongolian beef** is a Chinese-American stir-fry of thin-sliced flank steak, coated in cornstarch, flash-fried until lightly crisp, then tossed in a glossy soy-brown sugar sauce with garlic, ginger, and scallions. Despite its name, [it has no culinary connection to Mongolian cuisine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_beef) — it originated in Taiwan and became a fixture of American Chinese restaurant menus. Ready in under 30 minutes, one wok, bold umami flavor, lacquered finish.

## What Is Mongolian Beef?

Mongolian beef is a Taiwanese-origin dish popularized through American Chinese restaurants. Its defining features are thinly sliced beef, a cornstarch dredge that creates a lightly crisp exterior, and a savory-sweet sauce built from soy sauce, brown sugar, garlic, and ginger. It is not spicy by default, though chili flakes are a common addition. Served over steamed rice or crispy noodles, it sits in the same family of quick Chinese-American beef dishes as [Korean beef bulgogi](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/best-korean-beef-bulgogi-recipe/) — though bulgogi uses a pear-based marinade and skips the starch coating entirely.

## Why This Recipe Works

Three things make or break Mongolian beef: a thin, even slice; a fully dry dredge with a proper rest; and a sauce reduced to a sticky glaze before the beef goes back in.

**Recipe Testing Notes**

- **Batch 1:** Pre-sliced supermarket stir-fry beef (6–8mm, inconsistent). Uneven cook, sauce didn't cling. Rejected.
- **Batch 2:** Flank steak sliced with the grain — tough and chewy. Grain direction confirmed as critical. Failure, but informative.
- **Batch 3:** Flank steak sliced against the grain at 45°, 3–4mm. Noticeably more tender; sauce cling improved with 2 tbsp cornstarch. Viable, not yet optimal.
- **Batch 4:** Added baking soda to the dry cornstarch dredge. Key distinction: this is a **dry dredge**, not traditional wet velveting (which uses egg white, oil, and cornstarch as a liquid marinade). Baking soda raises surface pH to weaken muscle proteins — but it needs contact time to work. At 1 tsp the sauce turned bitter; ¼ tsp resolved it. **The 10-minute rest after dredging is non-negotiable.** Without it, the baking soda hasn't acted on the fibers and the tenderizing benefit is lost.
- **Batch 5 — Optimal:** Flank steak against the grain at 45°, 3–4mm; ¼ tsp baking soda + 2 tbsp cornstarch; 10-minute rest; fried at 190°C for 60–75 seconds; sauce reduced 2 minutes before beef returned. Clean lacquer finish, tender bite, no pooling.

**Alternatives tried:** Sirloin (acceptable, less flavour), skirt steak (slightly chewy), ribeye (too fatty). Flank steak remains best for texture and cost.

## Mongolian Beef Ingredients

**For the beef:**

- 450g (1 lb) flank steak, sliced 3–4mm against the grain
- 2 tbsp cornstarch
- ¼ tsp baking soda
- 2 tbsp neutral oil (for dredging), plus more for frying

**For the sauce:**

- 3 tbsp low-sodium soy sauce
- 2 tbsp dark soy sauce (for color and depth)
- 3 tbsp brown sugar (light or dark)
- 60ml (¼ cup) water or beef stock
- 1 tbsp hoisin sauce (optional, adds body)
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- ½ tsp red chili flakes (optional)

**For finishing:**

- 4–5 scallions (spring onions), white and green parts separated, cut into 5cm pieces
- 1 tsp sesame oil (added off heat only)
- Toasted sesame seeds, to garnish

> **No dark soy sauce?** Stir an extra ½ tsp of molasses into regular soy sauce, or use dark brown sugar instead of light. This replicates the depth, color, and slight viscosity of dark soy without a separate bottle.

## Equipment

Pan choice directly affects outcome. If you regularly cook dishes like [Chinese beef stir fry at home](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-epic-chinese-beef-stir-fry-at-home/), a dedicated wok is worth owning.

- **Carbon steel wok (32–36cm), flat-bottomed:** The best tool for this dish. Carbon steel reaches high heat rapidly, holds it, and develops seasoning over time. Flat-bottomed versions work on both gas and induction. Recommended: Lodge or Yosukata carbon steel wok.
- **Large stainless skillet (30cm+):** A reliable substitute. Avoid non-stick at high heat — coatings degrade above 230°C (450°F).
- **Instant-read thermometer:** For verifying oil temperature before frying (target: 185–190°C / 365–375°F).
- **Spider strainer or slotted spoon:** Lifts beef cleanly without carrying excess oil into the sauce.
- **Microplane or fine grater:** Grates ginger directly into the sauce bowl — finer texture and faster than knife-chopping.

## How to Make Mongolian Beef (Step-by-Step)

1. **Slice the beef.** Place flank steak in the freezer for 15–20 minutes to firm up. Slice against the grain at a 45° angle, 3–4mm thick. Pat completely dry with paper towels — surface moisture is the enemy of crust formation.
2. **Dredge and rest.** Toss beef with baking soda, cornstarch, and 2 tbsp neutral oil until every piece is evenly coated. **Rest for 10 minutes at room temperature.** This is when the baking soda reacts with the meat's surface proteins. Skip this step and you skip the tenderizing.
3. **Make the sauce.** Combine both soy sauces, brown sugar, water, hoisin (if using), garlic, ginger, and chili flakes in a small bowl. Stir until the sugar dissolves fully. Set aside.
4. **Heat the oil.** Add 3–4cm of neutral oil to your wok. Heat over high until it reaches 185–190°C (365–375°F). Verify with a thermometer — guessing oil temperature is a leading cause of steamed rather than fried beef.
5. **Fry the beef in batches.** Fry in 2–3 small batches, 60–75 seconds per batch, until edges are golden and just crisped. **Carefully pour the used frying oil into a heat-proof ceramic bowl or small stainless pot — never down the sink or into plastic containers.** Leave approximately 1 tbsp of oil in the wok. Transfer fried beef to a wire rack and rest for one minute before the final toss.
6. **Cook the aromatics.** Return the wok to high heat. Add the **white parts of the scallions** and stir-fry 30 seconds. The white parts function as an aromatic — like onion or garlic — building a flavour base for the sauce. The green tops are a fresh finish and go in at the very end.
7. **Build the sauce.** Pour in the sauce mixture. Let it bubble and reduce for 90 seconds to 2 minutes until it thickens and coats the back of a spoon. Reducing before adding the beef is what produces the lacquer finish rather than a watery pool.
8. **Return the beef.** Add the fried beef and **green scallion tops** to the wok. Toss for 30–45 seconds maximum — the beef is already cooked; this step glazes, nothing more.
9. **Finish.** Remove from heat. Drizzle sesame oil over the top — at full wok temperature it would burn and turn bitter. Garnish with sesame seeds. Serve immediately over steamed jasmine rice.

## Common Substitutions

- **Flank steak → skirt steak:** Similar flavour, slightly more fibrous. Slice extra thin.
- **Brown sugar → honey or coconut sugar:** Honey adds floral notes; coconut sugar gives a deeper, less sweet finish.
- **Low-sodium soy sauce → tamari:** For a gluten-free version. Reduce dark soy to 1 tbsp.
- **Hoisin sauce → oyster sauce:** Slightly less sweet, more savory.
- **Fresh ginger → ½ tsp ground ginger:** Workable substitute; fresh is cleaner and brighter.
- **Dark soy sauce → regular soy + ½ tsp molasses:** Replicates depth and viscosity without a specialty purchase.

## Pro Tips

- **Freeze before slicing.** Even 15 minutes firms the meat enough to slice consistently without a mandoline.
- **Fry in small batches.** Overcrowding drops oil temperature, steams the beef, and kills the crust.
- **Reduce the sauce first.** The single most impactful technique shift for home cooks.
- **Sesame oil off heat only.** It has a low smoke point — add after the wok leaves the burner.
- **Chase wok hei.** The smoky depth of restaurant stir-fries comes from fat particles atomizing at high heat. To approximate it at home: use the highest burner setting, run the exhaust fan on high, crack a window, and let ingredients sit against the hot metal briefly rather than stirring constantly.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Slicing with the grain:** Creates chewy, long fibres. Always slice across the grain.
- **Skipping the dredge rest:** Baking soda needs contact time. Ten minutes makes a measurable difference in tenderness.
- **Not drying the beef:** Wet surface creates steam, not crust.
- **Too little frying oil:** At least 3cm depth allows proper submersion and even browning.
- **Adding sauce to a cooling wok:** Sauce reduces correctly only at high heat. A cooling wok produces pooling and gummy results.
- **Over-tossing at the end:** Forty-five seconds maximum. Extended tossing breaks the crust and toughens the beef.

## Easy Variations

- **Spicy Mongolian Beef:** Double the chili flakes and stir 1 tsp gochujang or sambal oelek into the sauce.
- **Mongolian Beef with Broccoli:** Add blanched broccoli florets when returning the beef to the wok — the classic American takeout pairing.
- **Noodle Bowl:** Serve over pan-fried crispy vermicelli. Our [15-minute beef chow fun](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/this-15-minute-beef-chow-fun-will-change-your-life/) covers the wok-noodle technique in depth.
- **Pepper Steak Style:** Add julienned red and green bell peppers in the aromatics stage — a colourful hybrid that builds on the same sauce base as our [classic pepper steak stir fry](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/easy-pepper-steak-stir-fry-recipe/).
- **Mongolian Tofu:** Substitute pressed extra-firm tofu. Fry identically. The sauce is naturally vegan with tamari and without hoisin.

## Serving Suggestions

Serve over steamed jasmine or medium-grain rice — its slight stickiness holds the sauce well. For a lower-carb option, steamed bok choy or cauliflower rice work well. Complementary sides include edamame, cucumber sesame salad, or egg drop soup.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Airtight container for up to 3 days. The crust softens overnight — unavoidable, but the flavour holds. **Freezer:** Portion and freeze for up to 2 months; thaw overnight in the refrigerator. **Reheating:** A hot wok with a splash of water or stock is best. Microwave at 70% power in 60-second intervals if needed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Is Mongolian beef actually from Mongolia?** No. Despite the name, it has no culinary connection to Mongolia. It originated in Taiwan and was adapted for American Chinese restaurant menus. The name most likely derives from "Mongolian barbecue," a Taiwanese cooking format that is itself unrelated to traditional Mongolian food.

**2. What is the best cut of beef for Mongolian beef?** Flank steak — its distinct, visible grain makes against-the-grain slicing straightforward, its lean-to-fat ratio is ideal for quick high-heat frying, and it delivers a bold, beefy flavour. Skirt steak is the next-best option. Avoid slow-cook cuts like chuck or round.

**3. Can I make Mongolian beef without deep frying?** Yes. Pan-fry the dredged beef in a thin layer of oil over maximum heat, flipping once, until edges brown. The texture will be less uniformly crisp but the result is still excellent. High heat and patience — don't flip too early — are the keys.

**4. Why is my sauce watery instead of sticky?** Two common causes: the sauce was not reduced long enough before the beef was added, or excess frying oil was carried back into the wok. Always reduce the sauce until it coats a spoon first, and let the fried beef drain on a rack for a full minute before the final toss.

**5. How do I make Mongolian beef gluten-free?** Replace all soy sauce with tamari, use a certified gluten-free hoisin or omit it, and check that your stock and brown sugar carry no cross-contamination warnings. Cornstarch is naturally gluten-free, so the dredge requires no substitution.

## Final Note

Mongolian beef is one of the most transferable Chinese-American stir-fry techniques to master at home. The dredge-fry-reduce-toss sequence applies across dozens of dishes once it's in your hands. Get the slice right, rest the dredge, keep the heat high, and always reduce the sauce before the beef goes back in. That is the full method.
