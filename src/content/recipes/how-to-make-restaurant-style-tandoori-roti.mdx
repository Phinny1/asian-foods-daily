Tandoori Roti is a traditional North Indian whole-wheat flatbread characterized by its charred exterior and soft, chewy interior. While traditionally baked in a **tandoor** (clay oven) at temperatures exceeding 480°C (900°F), this recipe utilizes the **inverted tawa method** to achieve authentic blistering and smoky flavor on a standard stovetop.

![Perfectly charred Tandoori Roti with signature leopard spots, brushed with ghee on ceramic plate](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Tandoori-Roti-5-1536.webp)

## What Is Tandoori Roti?

Tandoori Roti is a staple flatbread of the Indian subcontinent. Unlike *Naan*, which is typically made with refined flour (Maida) and leavened with yeast, Tandoori Roti uses **Atta** (stone-ground whole wheat flour). It is prized for its rustic texture and the distinct smoky aroma imparted by the high-heat environment of a clay oven.

## Why This Recipe Works

- **High Hydration:** The inclusion of yogurt and warm water ensures a soft crumb that doesn't turn brittle.
- **The Glue Effect:** By applying water to one side of the roti, we create a vacuum seal against an **iron tawa**, allowing the bread to be inverted directly over a flame without falling.
- **Simulated Tandoor Heat:** Direct flame exposure triggers the **Maillard reaction** rapidly, creating the signature charred "leopard spots."

## Ingredients You’ll Need For Tandoori Roti

![Tandoori Roti ingredients mise en place: whole wheat flour (Atta), yogurt, salt, warm water, oil, and baking powder on bamboo cutting board](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Tandoori-Roti-6-1536.webp)

- **Atta (Whole Wheat Flour):** 2 cups (Preferably a high-protein brand like Aashirvaad Select).
- **Salt:** 1 tsp.
- **Sugar:** ½ tsp (to assist in browning).
- **Baking Soda:** ¼ tsp (provides a slight lift).
- **Yogurt (Dahi):** 2 tbsp (tenderizes the gluten).
- **Oil/Ghee:** 1 tbsp (for the dough) + extra for brushing.
- **Warm Water:** Approx. ¾ cup (adjust as needed).

## Equipment

- **Iron Tawa:** Essential. Non-stick pans will not work as the roti will slide off when inverted.
- **Rolling Pin (Belan):** For even thickness.
- **Gas Stovetop:** Required for the open-flame charring step.

## How to Make Tandoori Roti (Step-by-Step)

![Hand-kneading Tandoori Roti dough in glass bowl—mixing whole wheat flour with yogurt and warm water for soft, elastic texture](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Tandoori-Roti-4-1536.webp)

1. **Dough Preparation:** In a large bowl, whisk together the dry ingredients. Add yogurt and oil. Gradually incorporate warm water, kneading for 8–10 minutes until the dough is smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky.
2. **Resting (Crucial):** Cover the dough with a damp cloth and let it rest for at least **30–45 minutes**. This allows the gluten to relax and the flour to fully hydrate.
3. **Dividing:** Divide the dough into golf-ball-sized portions. Roll them into smooth rounds.
4. **Rolling:** Dust a ball with flour and roll it into a 6-inch circle. It should be slightly thicker than a standard *Phulka* or *Chapati*.
5. **The Water Seal:** Flip the rolled roti onto your palm. Generously brush the top side with water. Ensure no spots are dry.
6. **The First Sear:** Place the roti, water-side down, onto a **smoking hot iron tawa**. Press gently. Bubbles will begin to form within 30 seconds.
7. **The Inversion:** Once bubbles appear, pick up the tawa by the handle and flip it upside down directly over the high flame. Move the tawa in a circular motion 2 inches above the flame to char the edges and top evenly.
8. **Release:** Flip the tawa back. Use a spatula to scrape the roti off (it should be cooked on the bottom).
9. **Finishing:** Immediately brush with ghee or butter.

## Pro Tips for Success

- **Temperature Control:** The tawa must be extremely hot. If the roti doesn't stick, your tawa isn't hot enough or you didn't use enough water.
- **Avoid Non-Stick:** The Teflon coating prevents the dough from adhering, making the inversion step impossible.
- **Thickness Matters:** If rolled too thin, the roti will become a cracker; too thick, and it will remain raw in the center. Aim for 2–3mm.

![Rolled Tandoori Roti dough—6-inch circle, 2-3mm thick, slightly thicker than chapati for proper stovetop charring](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Tandoori-Roti-2-1536.webp)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using Cold Water:** Cold water tightens the gluten; always use lukewarm water for a softer result.
- **Skipping the Rest:** Failing to rest the dough results in "spring-back," making it difficult to roll and tough to eat.
- **Too Much Flour:** Excessive dusting flour during rolling will burn on the tawa and create a bitter taste.

## Easy Variations

- **Garlic Tandoori Roti:** Sprinkle minced garlic and fresh cilantro onto the dough before the final roll.
- **Missi Tandoori Roti:** Replace 1/3 of the Atta with Besan (chickpea flour) and add carom seeds (ajwain).
- **Butter Roti:** Increase the ghee brushing and wrap in foil immediately to steam-soften.

## Serving Suggestions

Tandoori Roti is best paired with heavy, gravy-based dishes that benefit from a sturdy bread:

- **Dal Makhani:** The creaminess complements the smoky wheat flavor.
- **Paneer Tikka Masala:** An iconic restaurant pairing.
- **Mutton Rogan Josh:** The roti holds up well to rich, oil-based curries.

## Storage and Reheating

- **Storage:** Wrap in a clean cloth and place in an airtight container for up to 24 hours.
- **Reheating:** Do not microwave (it will become rubbery). Reheat on a hot tawa for 15 seconds per side or wrap in foil and place in a 350°F oven for 5 minutes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I make this in an oven?** A: Yes. Pre-heat a pizza stone or baking tray at the highest setting (500°F/260°C). Place the roti on the stone and broil for 1–2 minutes until charred.

**Why is my roti getting hard?** A: This usually happens due to low hydration in the dough or cooking for too long on low heat. High heat and quick cooking are essential for softness.

**Is Tandoori Roti healthy?** A: Yes, it is generally healthier than Naan because it is made from whole wheat (fiber-rich) rather than refined flour. For more on the nutritional profile of ancient grains, see ++[Britannica's entry on Wheat](https://www.britannica.com/plant/wheat)++.
