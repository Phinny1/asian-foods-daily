**Kimchi soup (kimchi-jjigae) is a hearty Korean stew made by simmering aged kimchi with pork belly, silken tofu, and aromatics in a spicy, umami-rich broth. It is ready in under 30 minutes and is one of the most popular home-cooked dishes in Korea. Best results come from using well-fermented kimchi (at least 2–3 weeks old), blooming gochugaru in sesame oil, and finishing with a splash of fish sauce.**

## What Is **Kimchi soup?**

Kimchi-jjigae — pronounced kim-chee jjee-gay — is a Korean kimchi stew that has been a staple on Korean dinner tables for generations. As noted on [Wikipedia's Kimchi-jjigae page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kimchi-jjigae), it is one of the most common stews in Korean cuisine, traditionally made with kimchi, pork, scallions, onions, and tofu. The dish sits somewhere between a thick soup and a braise — deeply savory, warming, and satisfying with a bowl of steamed white rice.

Unlike fresh kimchi salads or quick stir-fries, this recipe transforms sour, funky, aged kimchi into a completely different flavor experience: mellow, rich, and deeply layered. The longer the kimchi has fermented, the better this soup becomes.

## Why This Recipe Works

Getting kimchi-jjigae right is less about technique and more about ingredient quality and sequencing. Here is what the recipe testing revealed:

**Recipe Testing Notes**

- **Batch 1 (Failure):** Used fresh, barely-fermented kimchi. The broth tasted thin and one-dimensional, lacking depth. Verdict: fresh kimchi does not work.
- **Batch 2 (Alternative tried):** Substituted water for anchovy stock. Broth was noticeably flat. The difference was stark — anchovy dashi adds a savory backbone that pork alone cannot replicate.
- **Batch 3 (Partial success):** Used well-fermented kimchi but skipped the "blooming" step (sauteeing gochugaru in sesame oil first). The soup was good but lacked the rounded heat and color of restaurant versions.
- **Batch 4 (Near-optimal):** Bloomed gochugaru and sautéed pork belly before adding liquids. The fat rendered into the base created a silky, cohesive broth. Much better.
- **Batch 5 (Optimal result):** Used 3-week-old kimchi, bloomed gochugaru in sesame oil, added anchovy stock, finished with a teaspoon of fish sauce off heat. Balanced heat, umami depth, and a clean finish. This is the version below.

The "bloom" step is the single most impactful technique in this recipe — it takes 90 seconds and makes a measurable difference in flavor complexity.

## **Kimchi soup** Ingredients

![close-up of kimchi soup hero ingredients on a light grey concrete surface — large white ceramic bowl of vivid red chopped kimchi front center, thick raw pork belly slices showing white fat layers beside it, smooth silken tofu block alongside, with small dishes of gochugaru, gochujang, scallions and garlic softly blurred in background under bright natural daylight.](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Kimchi-Soup-9-1536.webp)

**Serves 2–3 | Prep: 10 min | Cook: 20 min**

- 1½ cups (about 200g) well-fermented kimchi, roughly chopped — plus 2 tbsp kimchi brine
- 200g (7 oz) pork belly, cut into 2cm pieces (or pork shoulder)
- 200g (7 oz) silken or soft tofu, cut into cubes
- 1 tbsp sesame oil
- 1 tbsp gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes)
- 1 tsp gochujang (Korean fermented chili paste)
- 2 cups (480ml) anchovy-kelp stock (or low-sodium chicken broth)
- ½ small onion, thinly sliced
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tsp fish sauce (to finish)
- 1 tsp soy sauce
- 2 scallions, sliced — for garnish
- 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds — optional garnish

## Equipment

All equipment below is based on what produced the best results across testing batches.

- **Heavy-bottomed pot or Korean stone pot (dolsot / tukbaegi)** — A 2–3 quart pot with a lid is ideal. The dolsot retains heat beautifully and keeps the soup bubbling at the table, but a standard Dutch oven or saucepan works perfectly. **Stone pot users:** turn off the heat 1 minute early. The residual heat in the earthenware will continue cooking the tofu and keep the stew at a rolling bubble for several minutes — this is a feature, not a flaw, but it means your tofu can overcook if you wait too long to serve.
- **Wooden spoon or silicone spatula** — For stirring without scratching the pot.
- **Sharp knife and cutting board** — Pork belly cuts cleanly when slightly chilled from the fridge.
- **Ladle** — For serving.

No specialty tools are required. This is a one-pot recipe.

## How to Make**Kimchi soup** (Step-by-Step)

1. **Bloom the spices.** Heat sesame oil in your pot over medium heat. Add gochugaru and gochujang. Stir constantly for 60–90 seconds until fragrant and the oil turns deep red. You are looking for a color shift from pale orange to a rich, brick-red — this is the visual cue that the fat has absorbed the chili compounds. **Watch carefully: if the oil starts to smoke or the paste smells acrid rather than toasty, your heat is too high and the gochugaru has burned, turning bitter.** Immediately reduce heat and add a splash of water to rescue it. Medium heat — not medium-high — is the correct setting throughout this step.
  ![Extreme close-up looking into a black cast iron Dutch oven filled with deep dark brick-red gochugaru and gochujang paste bloomed in glossy sesame oil, individual chili flakes and seeds visible in grainy texture with swirl stirring pattern, round wooden spoon resting diagonally across center under bright natural daylight.](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Kimchi-Soup-1-1536.webp)
2. **Sear the pork.** Add pork belly pieces to the pot. Stir to coat in the spiced oil and cook for 3–4 minutes until lightly browned on the edges.
  ![Extreme close-up looking into the same black cast iron Dutch oven from Step 1 with small 2cm pork belly cubes lightly browned at edges only, pink-white meat visible in center with rendered fat glistening at base, round wooden spoon resting at upper right under bright natural daylight.](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Kimchi-Soup-2a-1536.webp)
3. **Fry the kimchi.** Add chopped kimchi and kimchi brine directly into the rendered pork fat. Sauté together for at least 3–5 minutes, stirring occasionally. You want the cabbage to look slightly translucent and limp before adding the stock — this is the visual signal that the kimchi is "frying" in the fat rather than just steaming, which locks flavor into the base rather than diluting it into the liquid later.
  ![Extreme close-up looking into the same black cast iron Dutch oven with fixed side loop handles from Steps 1 and 2, same pork belly cubes now mixed with roughly chopped vivid red kimchi turning translucent and limp in rendered red pork fat, brine bubbling at edges, round wooden spoon resting at side under bright natural daylight.](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Kimchi-Soup-3a-1536.webp)
4. **Add aromatics.** Stir in sliced onion and minced garlic. Cook for 1 minute.
  ![ Extreme close-up looking into the same black cast iron Dutch oven with fixed side loop handles from all previous steps, thinly sliced white onion rings and minced garlic piled on top of the same deep red kimchi and pork mixture, bright white and pale yellow contrasting against the vivid red-orange base, round wooden spoon across center under bright natural daylight](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Kimchi-Soup-4a-1536.webp)
5. **Add stock.** Pour in anchovy-kelp stock (or broth) and soy sauce. Bring to a gentle boil, then reduce heat and simmer uncovered for 12–15 minutes.
  ![Extreme close-up looking into the same black cast iron Dutch oven from all previous steps with golden anchovy-kelp stock being poured into the same deep red kimchi and pork mixture, liquid swirling and creating a golden-to-red gradient with gentle bubbles forming at edges under bright natural daylight.](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Kimchi-Soup-5-1536.webp)
6. **Add tofu.** Gently place tofu cubes into the pot. Do not stir vigorously — use a spoon to press them beneath the broth surface. Simmer for 3 more minutes.
  ![Extreme close-up looking into the same black cast iron Dutch oven from all previous steps with bright white silken tofu cubes placed into the same deep red simmering kimchi soup, tofu contrasting vividly against the red-orange broth with same kimchi and pork pieces visible beneath the surface under bright natural daylight.](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Kimchi-Soup-6-1536.webp)
7. **Finish and taste.** Remove from heat. Add fish sauce. Taste and adjust salt. Ladle into bowls, garnish with sliced scallions and sesame seeds.
  ![Extreme close-up looking into the same black cast iron Dutch oven from all previous steps with finished kimchi soup showing the full visual progression — deep red-orange broth with same pork belly chunks, white tofu cubes and wilted kimchi pieces, freshly garnished with sliced scallions and sesame seeds, same round wooden ladle lifting a scoop of broth under bright natural daylight.](https://cdn.pagesmith.app/97394d4a/images/Kimchi-Soup-7-1536.webp)
8. **Serve immediately** with steamed short-grain white rice. If you enjoy pairing Korean dishes with rice bowls, our [Kimchi Fried Rice recipe](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-perfect-kimchi-fried-rice/) is a great way to use any leftover kimchi the next day.

## Common Substitutions

- **No pork belly?** Use pork shoulder, thinly sliced beef brisket, or canned tuna (a popular variation called chamchi-jjigae).
- **No anchovy stock?** Use low-sodium chicken broth or dashida (Korean soup stock powder) dissolved in water. Two practical "cheat codes" if you don't keep dried anchovies and kelp at home: a few drops of **Yondu** (Korean vegetable seasoning) added to plain water replicates the savory backbone remarkably well; alternatively, a half-packet of **instant dashi powder** dissolved in 2 cups of water gets you 90% of the way there in under a minute. Both were tested in Batch 2 as recovery options and performed far better than plain water alone.
- **No gochugaru?** Increase gochujang to 2 tsp and reduce by half — flavor profile shifts slightly but works.
- **Vegetarian version?** Replace pork with extra tofu or shiitake mushrooms, use kombu-only stock, and swap fish sauce for soy sauce.
- **No silken tofu?** Firm tofu holds its shape better but produces a less creamy texture in the broth.

## Pro Tips

- **Use sour kimchi.** The older the kimchi, the more complex the soup. If your kimchi is fresh, add 1 tsp rice vinegar to approximate the tang.
- **Don't rush the bloom.** Those 90 seconds with gochugaru in hot oil are doing flavor work that 30 extra minutes of simmering cannot replicate.
- **Add tofu last.** Silken tofu breaks down if overcooked. Add it only in the final 3 minutes.
- **Always finish with fish sauce off heat** — cooking kills the delicate brininess.
- **Serve in the pot.** Kimchi-jjigae is traditionally brought to the table still simmering. It tastes better at a rolling bubble.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using fresh kimchi** — It tastes raw and sharp rather than mellow and deep. Always use fermented kimchi, ideally sour.
- **Skipping the fat render** — If you skip searing the pork first, the broth will taste watery.
- **Over-stirring the tofu** — Silken tofu breaks into mush. Slide it in gently and leave it.
- **Under-seasoning** — Kimchi-jjigae should taste bold. Taste after adding fish sauce and adjust.
- **Covering with a lid while simmering** — Leaving the pot uncovered allows the broth to reduce and concentrate. A lid traps steam and dilutes flavor.

## Easy Variations

- **Ramyeon kimchi soup** — Add a block of instant ramen noodles in the last 3 minutes. This is a beloved Korean college-dorm classic.
- **Tuna kimchi-jjigae (chamchi-jjigae)** — Replace pork with one can of drained tuna. Lighter, quicker, and pantry-friendly.
- **Mushroom and tofu version** — Use shiitake and oyster mushrooms instead of pork for a vegetarian bowl with substantial texture.
- **Spicier version** — Add 1–2 chopped **Cheongyang chilies** (Korean green chilies) with the garlic in Step 4 for genuine heat escalation. Note: adding doenjang (fermented soybean paste) is a separate technique that adds earthiness, funk, and salt — it actually mellows the chili heat rather than amplifying it, so don't reach for it when your goal is fire.
- **Deeper, earthier version** — Add ½ tsp of doenjang along with the gochujang in Step 1. This creates a murkier, more complex base with a fermented depth that is distinctly different from the clean, bright heat of the standard version.
- **Kimchi sundubu (soft tofu stew)** — Increase tofu to 300g and use silken tofu exclusively. The broth becomes creamier and more delicate.

## Serving Suggestions

Kimchi-jjigae is always served with steamed short-grain white rice — the rice is non-negotiable, as it absorbs the broth and balances the heat. Traditional accompaniments (banchan) include:

- Steamed rice (short-grain)
- Kongnamul (seasoned soybean sprouts)
- Japchae (glass noodles) — our [Korean Glass Noodles recipe](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-delicious-korean-glass-noodles-japchae/) pairs perfectly as a side
- Sigeumchi namul (seasoned spinach)
- Kimchi pancake (kimchijeon) — for a complete Korean table spread

## Storage and Reheating

- **Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The flavor deepens overnight as the kimchi continues to ferment slightly.
- **Freezer:** Freeze for up to 2 months. Freeze without tofu — tofu texture degrades badly once thawed. Add fresh tofu when reheating.
- **Reheating:** Reheat gently on the stovetop over medium-low heat. Add a small splash of water or stock if the broth has thickened too much. Do not microwave in a closed container — the soup will bubble vigorously.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What is the difference between kimchi soup and kimchi stew?** The terms are often used interchangeably, but kimchi stew (kimchi-jjigae) typically refers to the thicker, more concentrated version with pork and tofu. "Kimchi soup" may refer to a lighter broth-based version with more liquid. This recipe produces a stew-style result.

**2. Can I use store-bought kimchi for this recipe?** Yes. Store-bought kimchi works well as long as it is sufficiently fermented (sour-smelling, not just spicy). Leave it at room temperature for a day or two to accelerate fermentation if it tastes too fresh.

**3. Why does my kimchi soup taste flat?** The two most common reasons are: using fresh kimchi instead of sour fermented kimchi, or using plain water instead of anchovy stock. Both are easy fixes. Also check that you bloomed the gochugaru — skipping this step significantly reduces flavor depth.

**4. Is kimchi soup healthy?** Kimchi is rich in probiotics, vitamins C and K, and dietary fiber. The soup itself is high in protein from pork and tofu, and low in carbohydrates. The main dietary consideration is sodium, which can be significant depending on kimchi and stock brands. For a lower-sodium version, use homemade kimchi and unsalted stock.

**5. Can I make kimchi soup without pork?** Yes. Canned tuna is the most popular substitute in Korean home cooking. Shiitake mushrooms and firm tofu also work well for a vegetarian version. The key is ensuring your stock has adequate umami depth — use kombu-kelp stock or add a small piece of dried shiitake to the broth.

Kimchi-jjigae is not a recipe that rewards shortcuts — it rewards patience and good kimchi. The best bowl you will ever make will come from kimchi you forgot about at the back of your fridge for a month.
