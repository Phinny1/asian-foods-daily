**Arroz caldo** is a thick, ginger-forward Filipino rice porridge made by slowly simmering chicken pieces with glutinous rice in a seasoned broth until the rice breaks down into a creamy, congee-like consistency. To make it: sauté garlic, onion, and fresh ginger → add chicken and cook until golden → stir in glutinous rice → pour in broth and simmer for 30–35 minutes until thick and creamy → top with crispy garlic, boiled egg, scallions, and a squeeze of calamansi or lemon.

## What Is Arroz Caldo ?

Arroz caldo is one of the most beloved comfort dishes in Filipino cuisine. The name comes from Spanish — *arroz* means rice, *caldo* means broth — reflecting the lasting influence of over three centuries of Spanish colonial rule on Philippine food culture. As Smithsonian Magazine's exploration of [Filipino cuisine's layered cultural history](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/arts-culture/filipino-cuisine-asian-fusion-180954947/) notes, Filipino cooking absorbed Spanish, Chinese, Malay, and indigenous influences to create something entirely its own — and arroz caldo sits at the heart of that tradition.

At its core, the dish is the Filipino answer to Chinese congee: a warming, deeply savoury rice porridge built on chicken broth, fresh ginger, and fish sauce. It is the dish Filipinos reach for when someone is sick, when the weather turns cold, or when the body needs something uncomplicated and restorative. If you enjoy Filipino rice-based comfort food, the [classic Filipino chicken adobo recipe](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/filipino-chicken-adobo-recipe/) is a natural companion dish from the same culinary tradition — bold, savoury, and deeply satisfying.

**How the main Filipino rice porridge variations compare:**

Arroz caldo uses chicken as its protein, seasoned heavily with ginger and fish sauce, and is associated with sick days, cold weather, and home comfort. It is thick, creamy, and richly flavoured. Goto is the street-food cousin — structurally identical in technique but made with beef tripe instead of chicken, most commonly eaten at breakfast or from roadside stalls. The flavour is deeper and more mineral, and it is almost always served with tokwa't baboy (fried tofu and pork) on the side. Lugaw is the plainest of the three — a simple, minimally seasoned rice porridge with no protein base, used as a neutral canvas for toppings like chicharon, boiled eggs, or whatever is on hand. Chinese-style congee (jook) is the broader ancestor of all three: silkier in texture, more delicately seasoned, and typically served with a wider variety of toppings. Where Filipino versions lean into bold ginger and fish sauce, congee favours subtlety — for a direct comparison, the [authentic Cantonese congee recipe](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-the-best-cantonese-congee-ever/) shows exactly how the two traditions diverge.

## Why This Recipe Works

**Recipe Testing Notes:**

- **Batch 1 (Failure):** Used regular jasmine rice. Result: the grains stayed too firm and separate — the dish never achieved the creamy, porridge-like consistency that defines a good arroz caldo.
- **Batch 2 (Failure):** Added all the broth at once. Result: the rice absorbed unevenly and scorched at the bottom before the top had softened.
- **Batch 3 (Alternative tried):** Used bone-in chicken thighs only. Result: excellent depth of flavour and the bones enriched the broth naturally — a strong alternative to a whole chicken.
- **Batch 4 (Alternative tried):** Skipped the ginger bloom step (sautéing ginger before adding liquid). Result: flat, muted flavour — the ginger never properly opened up.
- **Batch 5 (Optimal):** Glutinous rice, chicken bloomed in the aromatics, broth added in two stages, ginger sautéed first. Result: silky, creamy porridge with deep ginger warmth and rich chicken flavour throughout.

**Why it works:** Glutinous rice breaks down as it cooks, releasing starch that thickens the broth naturally. Sautéing the ginger in oil before adding liquid unlocks its aromatic compounds. Adding broth in stages gives you control over final consistency.

## Arroz Caldo Ingredients

*(Serves 4)*

**For the porridge:**

- 1 lb (450g) bone-in chicken thighs or drumsticks
- 1 cup glutinous rice (malagkit), rinsed *(approximately 200g by weight — about the volume of a standard coffee mug)*
- 6 cups chicken broth, low-sodium
- 1 thumb-sized piece fresh ginger (about 2 inches), julienned
- 1 medium onion, diced
- 6 cloves garlic, minced (divided — 4 for the porridge, 2 for crispy topping)
- 2 tbsp fish sauce (*patis*), plus more to taste
- 1 tbsp cooking oil
- Salt and white pepper to taste

**Toppings:**

- 2 hard-boiled eggs, halved
- 3 scallions, sliced
- Calamansi or lemon wedges
- Crispy fried garlic
- Toasted garlic chips or chicharon (optional)

## Equipment *(Annotated Tool Recommendations Based on Testing)*

- **Heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven** — essential for even heat distribution; thin-bottomed pots caused scorching in Batch 2
- **Wooden spoon or heat-resistant silicone spatula** — for continuous stirring as the rice thickens; metal spoons scratch the pot
- **Ladle** — for adding broth in controlled stages
- **Small frying pan** — for frying the garlic topping separately without it burning in the main pot
- **Instant-read thermometer** *(optional)* — chicken is safe at 165°F / 74°C internally

## How to Make Arroz Caldo *(Step-by-Step)*

1. **Bloom the aromatics.** Heat oil in a heavy-bottomed pot over medium heat. Add julienned ginger and cook for 1–2 minutes until fragrant. Add onion and 4 cloves of minced garlic. Sauté for 3–4 minutes until softened and translucent.
2. **Brown the chicken.** Add chicken pieces and cook for 4–5 minutes per side until lightly golden. This builds fond on the bottom of the pot — don't skip it. Season with fish sauce and a pinch of white pepper.
3. **Add the rice.** Stir in the rinsed glutinous rice and coat it in the aromatics for 1–2 minutes. This toasting step adds a subtle nuttiness.
4. **Add broth in two stages.** Pour in 4 cups of broth first. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a steady simmer. Stir frequently — every 3–4 minutes — to prevent the rice from sticking to the bottom.
5. **Simmer until creamy.** Cook for 20 minutes, stirring regularly. As the porridge thickens, add the remaining 2 cups of broth gradually to reach your preferred consistency. Continue cooking for a further 10–15 minutes until the rice has fully broken down and the broth is thick and creamy.
6. **Adjust seasoning.** Taste and add more fish sauce, salt, or white pepper as needed. Remove chicken pieces, shred the meat off the bone, and return the meat to the pot.
7. **Make crispy garlic topping.** While the porridge finishes, fry the remaining 2 cloves of minced garlic in a small pan with a little oil over low heat until golden and crispy. Watch carefully — garlic burns quickly.
8. **Slice and serve.** Ladle into bowls and top with halved boiled eggs, sliced scallions, crispy garlic, and calamansi or lemon wedges on the side. These aren't optional garnishes — the crispy garlic adds essential texture contrast and the calamansi brightens the rich porridge. Serve immediately while hot.

> ⚠️ **Safety Note:** As the porridge thickens in Steps 4–5, it can scorch and spit hot starch. Reduce heat immediately if you smell burning, and stir with a long-handled spoon to keep hands away from steam. When shredding the chicken, use two forks or tongs — the meat and bones will be very hot. Allow chicken to rest for 2 minutes before handling.

## Common Substitutions

⭐ **Bone-in chicken thighs instead of a whole chicken** — same technique, same broth; thighs are easier to handle and still produce excellent flavour. Reduce total cooking time by 5 minutes.

- **Jasmine rice instead of glutinous rice** — the porridge will be less creamy and thinner; add an extra ½ cup of rice to compensate
- **Soy sauce instead of fish sauce** — use light soy sauce; the dish loses some umami depth but remains flavourful
- **Vegetable broth** — for a lighter version; add a strip of kombu for extra umami
- **Calamansi substitute** — fresh lemon juice or lime juice both work; calamansi is more floral but hard to source outside the Philippines

## Pro Tips

- **Never skip the ginger bloom** — sautéing ginger in oil before adding liquid is the single most important flavour step in this recipe. For more dishes that rely on this technique, the [authentic Cantonese congee recipe](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-the-best-cantonese-congee-ever/) uses a similar aromatic foundation.
- **Stir frequently once the rice thickens** — glutinous rice sticks aggressively as it cooks; stir every 3–4 minutes from the halfway point onwards
- **Make the crispy garlic topping separately** — adding raw garlic directly to the porridge at the end turns it bitter; always fry it in a separate pan
- **Arroz caldo thickens considerably as it cools** — if reheating, add a splash of broth or water to loosen it back to the right consistency

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Using the wrong rice — jasmine or long-grain rice will not break down the same way; glutinous rice is non-negotiable for authentic texture
- Adding all the broth at once — this makes it impossible to control consistency and risks scorching
- Under-seasoning — fish sauce is the backbone of the flavour; taste and adjust throughout cooking, not just at the end
- Rushing the simmer — the creamy texture comes from time and starch release, not from turning up the heat

## Troubleshooting

**Porridge is too thin** — the rice needs more time. Continue simmering uncovered, stirring frequently, until the broth reduces and thickens. Avoid adding more rice at this stage.

**Porridge is too thick** — add warm broth or water a ladle at a time, stirring between each addition, until you reach the right consistency.

**Rice is sticking to the bottom** — reduce heat immediately and stir vigorously, scraping the bottom of the pot. Switch to a lower burner setting and stir more frequently from this point.

**Flavour is flat** — bloom a small extra piece of fresh ginger in oil in a separate pan, then stir it into the porridge. Add fish sauce in small amounts until the flavour sharpens.

**Chicken is dry or stringy** — next time, remove the chicken from the pot earlier (after Step 5) before it overcooks while the rice finishes. Shred and return just before serving.

## Easy Variations

- **Goto** — substitute beef tripe for the chicken, a popular street-food version common in Manila
- **Arroz caldo with egg drop** — whisk 2 eggs and slowly drizzle into the hot porridge in the last minute of cooking for ribbons of cooked egg throughout
- **Instant pot version** — cook on high pressure for 15 minutes, then natural release for 10 minutes; stir well before serving as the rice settles at the bottom
- **Quick weeknight version** — use rotisserie chicken and pre-made broth; reduce cooking time to 25 minutes since the chicken is already cooked; similar in spirit to [easy Asian-style chicken rice bowls](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/asian-style-chicken-rice-bowls/) for busy nights

## Storage and Reheating

- **Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 3 days. The porridge will thicken considerably in the fridge.
- **Freezer:** Freeze in individual portions for up to 2 months. Freeze without the toppings — add those fresh when serving.
- **Reheating:** Reheat in a small pot over low-medium heat, adding broth or water a splash at a time and stirring frequently until it returns to the right consistency. Do not microwave from frozen — thaw overnight in the fridge first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between arroz caldo and lugaw?** Both are Filipino rice porridges, but arroz caldo uses chicken and is heavily seasoned with ginger and fish sauce, while lugaw is a plainer, simpler porridge typically made without meat and served as a base for other toppings. Goto is a third variation that uses beef offal.

**Can I use regular rice instead of glutinous rice?** You can, but the texture will be noticeably different — thinner and less creamy. Glutinous rice (malagkit) breaks down as it cooks and releases starch that thickens the broth naturally. If you must substitute, use a short-grain rice and increase the amount by about half a cup.

**Why does my arroz caldo taste bland?** The most common cause is under-seasoning with fish sauce. Fish sauce (*patis*) is the primary seasoning in this dish — without enough of it, the porridge tastes flat and one-dimensional. Taste and add fish sauce in small amounts until the flavour sharpens. A squeeze of calamansi or lemon just before eating also brightens everything considerably.

**Can arroz caldo be made ahead of time?** Yes, and it actually improves slightly overnight as the flavours meld. Make it up to 2 days ahead, store in the fridge without toppings, and reheat gently with a splash of broth. Add the crispy garlic, boiled eggs, and scallions only when serving.

**Is arroz caldo the same as Chinese congee?** They share the same basic concept — rice cooked down to a thick porridge in broth — but they differ in seasoning and toppings. Chinese congee (jook) tends to be more neutral with delicate toppings, while arroz caldo is more robustly seasoned with ginger and fish sauce and finished with bold garnishes. For a deeper look at the Chinese version, the [Cantonese congee recipe](https://www.asianfoodsdaily.com/recipes/how-to-make-the-best-cantonese-congee-ever/) shows exactly how the two approaches compare.
